# "Don't Die!": Performing Cybroc

*English translation of Amber's Chinese draft — March 2026*
*Translator: Biber*

**Key reference:** Neil Postman, *Amusing Ourselves to Death: Public Discourse in the Age of Show Business* (Viking Penguin, 1985). ISBN: 0670804541.

---

I have been afraid of dying for as long as I can remember.

More precisely, I was afraid of the night. During the day, death felt like a concept — a word in a textbook, something that happened to other people in movies. But at night, once the lights went out, the ceiling receded into darkness, my body grew heavy, and time stretched toward infinity. In those moments I would realize, with sudden clarity, that one day I would cease to exist entirely. Not fall asleep, not depart, not go somewhere else — but end. As a child I lacked the vocabulary to describe this sensation. I only knew that it kept me lying in bed, eyes open, each thought sharpening the next, each sharpening the fear. As I grew older, I acquired more respectable language for it: "existential anxiety," "death awareness," "ultimate concern." But stripped down, it was always the same sentence: I do not want to die.

Of course, no one wants to die. Humanity has spent millennia trying to manage this fact. Some believe in heaven after death, others in reincarnation, still others in the soul, or in the hope that history will remember something of who they were. Religion, mythology, art, monuments — so much of human culture amounts to finding a tolerable way to frame the reality of mortality. Death is too large. So large that people must invent things larger than themselves to contain it. God is one such container. Meaning is another.

## I

Now, technology is taking over this ancient enterprise.

My interest in Bryan Johnson is not because I genuinely care how much broccoli he eats each day, how many supplements he takes, what time he goes to sleep, or when he takes his cold plunge. What interests me is what he represents: a category of person no longer content to understand or accept death, but determined to route around it — to patch it like a system bug. Johnson is dead serious about this. He spends millions of dollars annually, assembles teams of physicians, tracks an extraordinary number of biomarkers, and executes precisely choreographed regimens of diet and exercise. He has transformed "not dying" from an ancient wish into a calendar, a spreadsheet, a content strategy, a lifestyle that borders on the religious. It is difficult to say whether he is practicing health management or enacting a new techno-theology. Formally, at least, his project already resembles the old religions — except this time, God is not in the heavens. God is on the dashboard.

## II

So we made *Cybroc*.

We did not write an essay to refute Johnson. We did not debate him, nor did we design a more sophisticated theoretical framework to dismantle his slogan. We did something simpler, and arguably stupider: we took several fresh heads of broccoli, fitted them with servo motors, mounting brackets, microcontrollers, and cheap mechanical assemblies, and made them perform Johnson's longevity protocol. Running, stretching, cold-water immersion, hanging, repetitive training. We transplanted an optimization impulse designed for the human body onto a vegetable. The broccoli suddenly began working out. It looked very dedicated, and very absurd — like an internet joke that went on too long, or a piece of performance art so earnest it circled back to comedy.

The "mechanical quality" of *Cybroc* does not come solely from the motors. The motors contribute only half. The other half comes from Blueprint itself. The logic of slicing life into time blocks, decomposing the body into parameters, translating aging into an interventionable workflow — all of this is already profoundly mechanical. It understands life as an executable script: when to wake, when to eat, when to run, when to recover, when to log. Once the broccoli was fitted with machinery, a strange doubling emerged within the work: is it the broccoli that resembles a machine, or have modern human beings simply been becoming machines all along? Sometimes I think we merely made something that was already happening a little more visible.

## III

In 2025, we brought *Cybroc* to the ALIFE conference exhibition in Kyoto, Japan. The story begins in a supermarket. The supermarket was called LIFE. We purchased the broccoli for *Cybroc* at a large Kyoto supermarket named LIFE. This detail felt like an over-engineered joke, as though fate had arranged a low-grade pun on our behalf: we went to LIFE to buy life, then carried it to an artificial life exhibition and wired it up with cables and motors. For a brief moment, I genuinely felt like Nüwa, the goddess who shaped humans from clay — except instead of molding earth, I was crouched in a corner of the exhibition hall, using zip ties, hot glue, and servo motors to give broccoli form, motion, and a deeply questionable kind of "vitality." The process was both wondrous and cheap — full of vegetables, plastic, electronic components, and improvised construction. Life, here, was not created so much as screwed on.

On the first day, the broccoli looked respectable. The florets were green, upright, even faintly ambitious. Visitors came by, laughed, took photographs, gathered around, asked questions. Someone asked whether it qualified as artificial life. It was a brilliant question, because the answer clearly depended on when you asked it. On day one, the broccoli appeared full of vitality. By day two, it had begun to yellow. By day three, it had started to smell. Once time entered the equation, the question changed entirely. Being alive is not a static judgment but a condition that continuously slips away.

On the second day, things took a turn — literally. The broccoli performing the cold-water immersion was the first to deteriorate. Repeated submersion and removal left white mycelium forming at its base, the stem softening, the edges discoloring. The one on the treadmill fared no better: the crown lost its firmness, and its exercise posture shifted from "working out" to "struggling." The machines continued to operate according to their programs, but the movements increasingly resembled a kind of futile caregiving. The protocol, it became clear, does not actually care about life. It simply runs. Whether its subject benefits, or even persists, falls outside its scope of concern.

By the third day, a smell of rotting vegetable had settled over the exhibition space. The broccoli sagged, browned, seeped moisture; mold appeared in places. The servos kept pushing, the belts kept turning, the stretching motions kept repeating. At that moment the work finally arrived at its most precise state: a vegetable destined to rot, rigorously executing a longevity training regimen. The image was more parable-like than any text could be. Humans try to drag themselves out of time through discipline, technology, money, and data, while time watches all of this, says nothing, and simply continues forward.

## IV

But the story did not end with decomposition. Just as the broccoli was deteriorating, something else began to come alive. We had been livestreaming the entire process. Camera, ring light, laptop, plus a title the internet would love. Viewers arrived and watched broccoli run on treadmills, watched it gradually spoil under longevity training. Some laughed, some lurked, some left comments, some sent tips. Things quickly became deeply absurd: physical life was decaying while its digital life was inflating. The broccoli was dying; the content was growing. Attention kept flowing in, tips kept accumulating, and eventually it extended into memes, a token launch, imitations. It was a strange feeling, as though we had inadvertently witnessed a migration of "life" in our era: life need not reside in organic matter — it might exist in a traffic curve, in a chat room, in a string of symbols endlessly replicating themselves.

## V

The most remarkable part happened outside the exhibition, and inside the stomach.

We later used the livestream earnings to eat an extravagant kaiseki meal in Kyoto. In retrospect, the scene is almost too perfect, as though arranged by an overly clever novelist: on one hand, we were making a work about "not dying"; on the other, we were spending its proceeds on a cuisine that insists on seasonality, transience, and the unrepeatable. Kaiseki is fragile. It values the present moment, the irreproducible, the understanding that this bite is this bite, this season is this season, this encounter will not come again. It does not resist impermanence — it plates impermanence carefully and invites you to eat it. And so the broccoli, having struggled against aging in the exhibition hall, was ultimately converted into our capacity to consume impermanence at a Kyoto dinner table. We used a farce about life extension to purchase a feast about the beauty of brevity.

## VI

What *Cybroc* reveals is not some distant future but a reality already underway: within the system woven together by technology, capital, and entertainment platforms, life is no longer merely a biological question — it is a performance that can be trained, displayed, traded, and consumed. Humans resist death while converting death into content. They yearn for longevity while surrendering life itself to protocols and markets. Perhaps the road to the transhuman does not begin with steel prosthetics or digital immortality, but with the moment we willingly came to understand ourselves as systems awaiting continuous optimization.

When death anxiety is outsourced to technology, when being alive is outsourced to a protocol, when attention repackages decomposition as entertainment and revenue — are we actually extending life, or merely extending a performance of life? Are we truly closer to not dying, or have we simply become better at turning decay into content?

As a child, I feared death because I believed it was the end. Later, I came to suspect that something more frightening than death itself is a person's willingness, in the pursuit of not dying, to train themselves into a program that never goes offline. The broccoli, of course, died. It did not win. But before it spoiled, it completed its running, stretching, and cold-water immersion. It completed the livestream, the tips, the entertainment, the capital conversion, and ultimately treated us to a very good meal. Seen this way, it may conform more closely to the contemporary definition of "being alive" than many people do.

Don't die.
The broccoli says.
And then it rots.
