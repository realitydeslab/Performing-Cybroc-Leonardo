## 1. ATTENTION ECONOMY AND LIVE-STREAMING AS CULTURAL PHENOMENON

### Core Theoretical Texts

**Tim Wu, *The Attention Merchants: The Epic Scramble to Get Inside Our Heads* (Knopf, 2016)**
Wu traces the commodification of human attention from nineteenth-century penny newspapers through radio, television, and into the digital age. His central thesis is that media companies operate as "attention brokers" between two markets with different currencies -- money on one side, attention on the other. For Cybroc, Wu's framework is directly applicable: the broccoli cyborgs function as absurdist attention merchants, harvesting viewer gaze and converting it into donations. The absurdity of the performer (a vegetable) exposes what Wu implies but never states explicitly -- the attention economy is structurally indifferent to the content of what captures attention. If viewers will donate to watch broccoli exercise, the apparatus of attention capture has been laid bare.

**Yves Citton, *The Ecology of Attention* (Polity, 2017; orig. French 2014)**
Citton challenges the narrow economic framing of attention, arguing it should be conceptualized as an *ecology* rather than an *economy*. Drawing on Gregory Bateson, Felix Guattari, and Spinoza, he treats attention as relational and environmentally conditioned. He situates his work within the "burgeoning field of attention studies" alongside Pierre Levy, Jonathan Crary, Bernard Stiegler, Jonathan Beller, Franco Berardi, Dominique Boullier, and Matteo Pasquinelli. For Cybroc, Citton's ecological framing is productive: the live-stream creates an attention ecology in which human viewers, vegetable performers, mechanical actuators, and donation algorithms form a distributed system. The broccoli does not "seek" attention; the ecology produces it regardless.

**Jonathan Crary, *24/7: Late Capitalism and the Ends of Sleep* (Verso, 2013)**
Crary argues capitalism demands continuous availability, eroding the last remaining non-monetized human activity: sleep. Johnson's Blueprint epitomizes Crary's 24/7 subject -- a body optimized around the clock, sleeping at precisely calculated hours, never truly "off." The live-stream format extends this logic to the broccoli that, during the installation, never sleeps and is always available for viewing. Crary's observation that "24/7 is a time of indifference" maps onto the live-stream's indifference to whether anyone is watching.

**Bernard Stiegler, *Taking Care of Youth and the Generations* (Stanford UP, 2010) and "Pharmacology of Attention"**
Stiegler treats digital technologies as *pharmakon* -- simultaneously poison and remedy. He argues that current digital technologies in service of the market are a "major factor in the destruction of attention," producing what he calls "proletarianization of knowledge." For Cybroc, Stiegler's pharmacological lens is apt: the live-stream is both the medium of critique (making visible the attention economy's absurdity) and complicit in the very attention-capture apparatus it satirizes.

**Jonathan Beller, *The Cinematic Mode of Production: Attention Economy and the Society of the Spectacle* (Dartmouth College Press, 2006)**
Beller proposes that cinema and its digital successors "brought the industrial revolution to the eye," positioning looking itself as labor. His "attention theory of value" extends Marx by arguing that attention is the new form of productive labor in cognitive capitalism. For Cybroc, Beller's framework reframes viewers' watching as unwaged labor: their attention literally produces the economic value (donations) that funds the Kaiseki dinner. The audience labors; the artists eat.

### Live-Streaming Scholarship

**Mark R. Johnson and Jamie Woodcock, "'And Today's Top Donator is': How Live Streamers on Twitch.tv Monetize and Gamify Their Broadcasts," *Social Media + Society* 5(4), 2019**
The most directly relevant empirical study of live-stream donation culture. Johnson and Woodcock present a seven-part typology of Twitch monetization (subscribing, donating/"cheering," advertising, sponsorships, competitions/targets, unpredictable rewards, integrated games). They find donation cultures operate as *do ut des* exchanges -- viewers donate for recognition and parasocial intimacy. Crucially, they document viewers competing to be "top donator," donating hundreds or thousands of dollars in a gamified escalation. In Cybroc, this logic is inverted: viewers donate to a nonhuman, non-sentient performer incapable of reciprocating recognition. The parasocial relationship is with an organism that cannot know it has one.

**Zhang Ge and Xiang Yu, "Virtual Gifting on China's Live Streaming Platforms: Hijacking the Online Gift Economy," *Chinese Journal of Communication* 12(3), 2019, pp. 340-355**
The foundational peer-reviewed study on Chinese live-streaming gifting culture. Zhang and Xiang examine virtual gifting on platforms like Douyin and Kuaishou, analyzing the "complex dynamics between the gift economy and the commodity economy in the context of increasingly platformized Chinese society." They find that platform infrastructure is "geared toward maximizing content monetization through virtual gifting" and that corporatized streamer guilds "foreground the moneymaking capacity of virtual gifting at the expense of its potential for building communitarian and reciprocal relationships." The study documents how only 4% of monthly active viewers on Douyu send paid virtual gifts, yet 2.7% of high-value viewers contribute 80.2% of total virtual gift value -- a power-law distribution. For Cybroc's economic commentary, this extreme skew is relevant: the donation economy is structurally unequal, and the installation makes this visible.

**Wohn, D.Y., "The One-and-a-Half Sided Parasocial Relationship: The Curious Case of Live Streaming," *Computers in Human Behavior* 133, 2022**
Argues that live-streaming creates a new form of parasocial relationship that is neither fully one-sided (as in television) nor genuinely reciprocal. Wohn calls this "one-and-a-half sided" because streamers can acknowledge individual viewers' chat messages and donations, creating a simulacrum of personal recognition without genuine relationship. For Cybroc, the parasocial relationship is reduced to zero-sided: the broccoli cannot even simulate recognition, yet the economic apparatus functions nonetheless.

**Additional live-streaming references:**
- Lee, Y.C. et al., "Understanding Gift-Giving in Game Live Streaming on Douyu: An Evaluation of PSR/Social Presence," *Frontiers in Psychology* 13, 2022. Finds parasocial relationships significantly influence donation intentions.
- Xu, X. et al., "Understanding Virtual Gifting in Live Streaming by the Theory of Planned Behavior," *Human Behavior and Emerging Technologies*, 2022. Examines decision-making factors in virtual gifting.
- "Beyond Livestreaming: The Rise of Social Media Gifting and Paid Memberships -- A Systematic Literature Review," *Journal of Business Research* 183, 2024. Comprehensive review of 76 articles across 51 journals.
- "The Spread of Virtual Gifting in Live Streaming: The Case of Twitch," *arXiv* 2501.09235v1, 2025. Documents how individuals imitate high-status donors to associate with higher social rank.

### Literature Gap
Almost no scholarship connects attention economy theory to wellness culture, biohacking, or health optimization content. The live-streaming of health-adjacent performance (even satirically, through broccoli cyborgs) sits at an unexamined intersection. The paper can argue that Cybroc reveals the attention economy's ultimate indifference -- the content need not even be alive in any meaningful sense; the infrastructure of capture operates regardless.

## 2. THE SELF-CONSUMING ECONOMIC CIRCUIT IN ART

### Relational Aesthetics and Its Critics

**Nicolas Bourriaud, *Relational Aesthetics* (Les Presses du Reel, 1998/2002)**
Bourriaud defines art as creating social environments for shared activities and "interstices" that "elude the capitalist economic context." The Kaiseki dinner funded by Cybroc's donation stream is a relational artwork -- a shared meal directly output by the performance circuit. However, unlike Rirkrit Tiravanija's paradigmatic *Untitled (Free)* (1992), where Thai curry was served freely to strangers, Cybroc's meal is funded by others' donations and consumed exclusively by the artists. This inversion is conceptually crucial.

**Claire Bishop, "Antagonism and Relational Aesthetics," *October* 110, Fall 2004, pp. 51-79**
Bishop's devastating critique charges relational art with "collapsing into compensatory entertainment" and ignoring institutional complicity with capitalism. She argues Bourriaud's artists produce "feel-good" social encounters that avoid genuine antagonism. Cybroc responds to Bishop's critique by making its economic apparatus visible rather than concealed -- the donation-to-dinner pipeline is the artwork's stated content, not its hidden infrastructure.

**Stewart Martin, "Critique of Relational Aesthetics," *Third Text* 21(4), 2007, pp. 369-386**
Martin argues Bourriaud's theory is "helplessly reversible into an aestheticisation of capitalist exchange." Cybroc's economic circuit deliberately stages this reversibility: the artwork does not pretend to be outside capitalism but performs capitalism's logic to the point of absurdity.

### Institutional Critique and Economic Systems

**Andrea Fraser, "From the Critique of Institutions to an Institution of Critique," *Artforum* 44(1), September 2005**
Fraser's key essay argues that institutional critique has evolved from external attack on institutions to self-reflexive analysis of art's own embeddedness in economic systems. She insists that "we are the institution" -- there is no pure position outside it from which to critique. Cybroc enacts this by making the artists themselves the beneficiaries of the economic circuit they critique.

**Hans Haacke** -- particularly *Shapolsky et al. Manhattan Real Estate Holdings, a Real-Time Social System, as of May 1, 1971* (1971) and later corporate sponsorship exposures. Haacke made visible the "largely abstract and invisible forces and relations that traverse particular social spaces." Cybroc extends Haacke's method by making its own economic circuit visible rather than that of an external institution.

**Gregory Sholette, *Dark Matter: Art and Politics in the Age of Enterprise Culture* (Pluto Press, 2011)**
Sholette examines the "dark matter" of artistic production -- the vast majority of creative labor that sustains the art world's economy without receiving recognition or compensation. His framework illuminates Cybroc's position: the broccoli is the ultimate dark-matter laborer, performing without agency, consciousness, or compensation, while producing real economic value. The phrase "artistic dark matter" describes "the marginalised and systematically underdeveloped aggregate of creative productivity that nonetheless reproduces the material and symbolic economy of high art."

### Bataille's General Economy: The Accursed Share

**Georges Bataille, *The Accursed Share, Volume I: Consumption* (Zone Books, 1988; orig. French 1949)**
Bataille's "general economy" theory is perhaps the single most productive theoretical framework for analyzing Cybroc's economic circuit. Bataille argues that every system produces excess energy (the "accursed share") that must be expended -- either through luxury, art, sacrifice, and festival, or catastrophically through war. His theory, drawn from Marcel Mauss's analysis of potlatch, proposes that "the accursed share is that excessive and non-recuperable part of any economy which must either be spent luxuriously and knowingly without gain in the arts, in non-procreative sexuality, in spectacles and sumptuous monuments, or it is obliviously destined to catastrophic outpouring in war." The Kaiseki dinner is precisely this: the donation surplus expended in luxurious, ephemeral consumption that produces nothing durable. The connection to Debord is also significant -- the Situationist International's bulletin was literally called *Potlatch*, named after the pre-commercial form of gift circulation.

**Ashley Lee Wong, *Ecologies of Artistic Practice: Rethinking Cultural Economies through Art and Technology* (MIT Press, 2025)**
Wong explores artists' economic relationships at the nexus of art and technology in a neoliberal creative economy. She argues for shifting "away from notions of individual authorship and finite artworks that can be bought and sold, and instead toward an understanding of artistic practices as collaborative, social, and cultural processes." Her examination of how artworks circulate through galleries, digital platforms, NFTs, grants, and residencies provides a contemporary framework for understanding Cybroc's unusual economic circuit.

### The Kaiseki Dimension: Food in Art Practice

**Rirkrit Tiravanija** -- *Untitled (Free)* (1992) and subsequent cooking works at galleries worldwide. Tiravanija's practice uses food to create situations for "communicative actions" (following Habermas). Scholarly analysis in Nicolaj Kierkegaard, "Food as a Medium Between Art and Cuisine: Rirkrit Tiravanija's Gastronomic Installations," in *Culinary Turn: Aesthetic Practice of Cookery* (transcript Verlag, JSTOR). The critical distinction: Tiravanija serves food freely and egalitarianly to strangers; Cybroc's dinner is funded by others and consumed exclusively by the artists.

**Daniel Spoerri** -- Founded the Eat Art Gallery (1970) in Dusseldorf above his Restaurant Spoerri (1968). His *tableaux pieges* ("snare pictures") fixed the detritus of a meal in place as art. Spoerri's work transforms the *aftermath* of consumption into art; Cybroc transforms the *funding* of consumption into art.

**Gordon Matta-Clark, FOOD Restaurant (1971-1974, SoHo, New York)** -- An artists' cooperative where different artists cooked each day, making "the act of cooking and the meals themselves a performative art." FOOD operated as both business and "Food Theatre." Unlike Cybroc, FOOD was genuinely cooperative; Cybroc's circuit is more ironically closed.

**"Feast: Radical Hospitality in Contemporary Art"** exhibition (Smart Museum, University of Chicago) -- Presented 30+ artists who transformed the shared meal into artistic medium, examining roots in early twentieth-century European avant-garde art. Provides the exhibition/curatorial genealogy for food-as-art.

**Kaiseki as Aesthetic/Philosophical Practice:**
- "The Aesthetics of Kaiseki: The Cultural and Philosophical Significance of Food Presentation in Japanese Fine Dining," *Art and Society* (Paradigm Press, 2024). Documents Kaiseki's origins in Zen Buddhist temple cooking, its embrace of *shun* (seasonal peak), *omotenashi* (hospitality), and Zen-inspired simplicity.
- Graham Parkes, "Japanese Aesthetics," *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* (2023 revision). Authoritative treatment of *mono no aware*, *wabi-sabi*, and *mujo*.
- The conceptual richness of using Kaiseki specifically: a multi-course haute cuisine rooted in Zen Buddhism where each course is a temporary composition designed to be consumed -- each dish vanishes in the act of appreciation, making it a perfect metaphor for the entire Cybroc circuit.

### Gift Economy in Art

**Lewis Hyde, *The Gift: Creativity and the Artist in the Modern World* (Vintage, 1983/2007)**
Greatly influenced by Marcel Mauss's *The Gift* (1925), Hyde argues that artistic creation operates within a gift economy fundamentally different from capitalist exchange. The "essence of creative vocations is 'erotic'" -- driven by passion and reciprocal generosity rather than profit. Hyde's framework illuminates what Cybroc disrupts: the live-stream donation ostensibly operates as gift (viewers voluntarily give) but is structurally embedded in capitalist platform architecture. The gift is "hijacked" (to use Zhang and Xiang's term) by the attention economy.

**Marcel Mauss, *The Gift: Forms and Functions of Exchange in Archaic Societies* (1925)**
Mauss's foundational analysis of gift exchange identifies three obligations: to give, to receive, and to reciprocate. The Cybroc circuit disrupts all three: the broccoli cannot give, cannot meaningfully receive, and certainly cannot reciprocate. Yet the economic exchange proceeds. This reveals that digital donation culture has severed the gift from its social-relational foundations.

### Literature Gap
No existing artwork combines the specific closed-loop economic circuit of Cybroc: live organism performs > earns real money through live-stream donations > money funds ephemeral luxury dining > dinner is consumed and vanishes. The closest parallels (Klein's gold in the Seine, Tiravanija's free meals, Sierra's paid labor) each lack one or more elements. The "self-consuming" economic circuit as an explicit artistic strategy has no established theoretical framework -- this is a contribution the paper can make.

## 3. ARTISTS USING ECONOMIC TRANSACTIONS, MONEY, OR CAPITALISM AS MEDIUM

### Primary Comparable Artworks

**Santiago Sierra** -- Paying marginalized workers to perform degrading or meaningless tasks in gallery settings (e.g., *Workers Who Cannot Be Paid, Remunerated to Remain Inside Cardboard Boxes*, 2000; *250 cm Line Tattooed on 6 Paid People*, 1999). Scholarly analysis in Julia Bryan-Wilson, "Santiago Sierra's Workers Who Cannot Be Paid: Precarious Labor in Contemporary Art," in *Bodies That Still Matter* (Cambridge UP). Sierra makes human exploitation visible by replicating it within the gallery; Cybroc substitutes broccoli for the exploited laborer -- if viewers will pay to watch vegetables exercise, what does this reveal about the attention economy's indifference to the content of labor?

**Yves Klein, *Zone de Sensibilite Picturale Immaterielle* (1959-1962)** -- Klein sold "zones of immaterial pictorial sensibility" (empty space) in exchange for gold leaf. Buyers could choose to burn their receipt while Klein threw half the gold into the Seine, completing a transaction in which both object and payment vanished. Both Klein and Cybroc create circuits culminating in the destruction of economic value -- Klein's gold dissolves into the Seine; Cybroc's donation money dissolves into a meal.

**Caleb Larsen, *A Tool to Deceive and Slaughter* (2009)** -- A black box perpetually self-auctioning on eBay. The artwork-as-economic-engine with the opposite temporal structure to Cybroc: perpetual circulation without consumption (Larsen) versus a circuit that terminates in consumption (Cybroc). The work "combines Robert Morris' *Box With the Sound of Its Own Making* with Baudrillard's writing on the art auction."

**Lee Lozano, *Dropout Piece* (1970)** -- Lozano's withdrawal from the art world, which she carried out as a conceptual artwork. Scholarly analysis: Jo Applin, "Hard Work: Lee Lozano's Dropouts," *October* 156, 2016 (MIT Press); Sarah Lehrer-Graiwer, *Lee Lozano: Dropout Piece* (Afterall Books, 2014). Lozano's radical refusal of art-world economics -- "withdrawal *is* the aim" -- represents the opposite strategy to Cybroc's explicit engagement with economic circuits. Where Lozano withdraws, Cybroc deliberately enters.

**J.S.G. Boggs (1955-2017)** -- Drew meticulous one-sided reproductions of banknotes and attempted to spend them at face value. His practice investigated the social construction of monetary value: if a waitress accepts his drawing of a $100 bill as payment, the drawing *is* money. Arrested for counterfeiting multiple times; acquitted in England in 1986 (defended by Geoffrey Robertson QC). His work is now in the British Museum. For Cybroc, Boggs's relevance lies in exposing value as social construct -- if viewers assign monetary value to watching broccoli exercise, the donation *is* the artwork's output.

**Mierle Laderman Ukeles, *Maintenance Art* series (1969-present)** -- Her 1969 *Manifesto for Maintenance Art* and subsequent performance works (washing museum floors, shaking hands with every NYC sanitation worker) exposed the invisible labor sustaining institutions. Her distinction between "development" work (valued, creative) and "maintenance" work (devalued, repetitive) applies directly to Cybroc: the broccoli performs maintenance-like repetitive exercise, yet this labor generates real economic value through the attention economy.

### Crypto/NFT Art Critique

**Rhea Myers, *Proof of Work: Blockchain Provocations 2011-2021* (Urbanomic/MIT Press, 2023)** -- Myers's pioneering blockchain art remodels "the tropes of conceptual art and net.art to explore what blockchain technology reveals about our concepts of value, culture, and currency." She is notably critical of how capitalism recuperates artistic critique: "Capitalism enjoys it when you resist, because that's how it finds new things to enclose. Capital grows by absorbing critique, by recuperating alterity." This self-awareness about critique's recuperation is directly relevant to Cybroc's self-consuming logic.

**Simon Denny** -- Appropriates "the aesthetics, mechanisms, and cosmology of the neoliberal economy and technosphere" to make them legible. Works like *Secret Power* (Venice Biennale, 2015) and subsequent blockchain/NFT installations use corporate aesthetics as medium. "Like Fred Wilson, an early practitioner of institutional critique who uses the conventions of the museum to subvert it, Denny uses the tools of the tech trade to question technology's promise of progress."

**Additional relevant artists:**
- **Danh Vo** -- purchases and displays historical objects, making the economics of acquisition visible
- **Cameron Rowley** -- creates financial instruments and contracts as artworks
- **Hito Steyerl** -- *Duty Free Art* (Verso, 2017) examines art in the age of planetary civil war and free ports
- **Wafaa Bilal** -- *3rdi* (2010) and auction-based works engaging with surveillance and economic exchange

## 4. ROGER CAILLOIS'S THEORY OF MIMICRY

### *Man, Play and Games* (1961)

**Roger Caillois, *Man, Play and Games* (1961; trans. Meyer Barash, University of Illinois Press, 2001)**
Caillois's four-category typology of play:
- **Agon** (competition) -- the implied competition with Johnson's metrics
- **Alea** (chance) -- the audience's unpredictable donations
- **Mimicry** (simulation/role play) -- the broccoli's mimicry of exercise
- **Ilinx** (vertigo) -- the overall vertiginous absurdity of the installation

Caillois defines mimicry as "when the individual plays to believe, to make himself or others believe that he is different from himself." Critically, Cybroc operates across *all four categories simultaneously* -- a rare intersection that Caillois himself suggested was structurally unusual. He considered mimicry-alea as a "forbidden" pairing, yet Cybroc combines mimicry (the broccoli's simulation of exercise) with alea (the unpredictable donation economy).

**Scholarly analyses:**
- Thomas S. Henricks, "Caillois's Man, Play, and Games: An Appreciation and Evaluation," *American Journal of Play* 3(2), 2010. Comprehensive scholarly assessment.
- Peter D. McDonald, "The Principle of Division in Roger Caillois's Man, Play and Games," *Games and Culture* 15(8), 2020. Examines the structural logic of Caillois's categories.

### "Mimicry and Legendary Psychasthenia" (1935)

**Roger Caillois, "Mimicry and Legendary Psychasthenia," *Minotaure* 7, 1935; reprinted in *October* 31, Winter 1984, pp. 17-32**
This is the more powerful of Caillois's two mimicry texts for the Cybroc paper. Against Darwinian utilitarian explanations, Caillois argues that insect mimicry is not adaptive but represents a **"dangerous luxury"** -- an excess revealing a drive toward dissolution of the self into environment. Drawing on Pierre Janet and Eugene Minkowski's phenomenological psychiatry, he describes mimicry as "depersonalization through assimilation into space" -- the organism "no longer knows where to place itself." Mimicry is "an incantation fixed at its culminating point and having caught the sorcerer in his own trap."

**Application to Cybroc:** The recursive mimicry chain creates precisely the dissolution Caillois theorizes:
1. Broccoli mimics Johnson's exercise protocols
2. Johnson mimics "natural health" (which is already a cultural construct)
3. "Natural health" mimics "nature" (which is itself a sign-system)
4. Each layer dissolves the distinction between model and copy

The broccoli performing human exercises is a literalization of Caillois's "dangerous luxury" -- mimicry in excess of any survival function, performed by an organism that cannot benefit from the imitation. The mimicry is pathological in Caillois's precise sense: it represents a "weakening of the soul" (*psychasthenia*), a loss of self-boundary that maps onto the biohacking subject's surrender of autonomous embodiment to algorithmic protocols.

### Contemporary Extensions of Caillois

**Nidesh Lawtoo, *Homo Mimeticus: A New Theory of Imitation* (Leuven University Press, 2022)**
The most significant contemporary extension of mimicry theory. Lawtoo's ERC-funded project (2017-2022) proposes that humans are fundamentally "mimetic creatures" whose consciousness is shaped by imitation. He extends mimetic theory to the digital age, arguing that "from imitation to identification, mimicry to affective contagion, techne to simulation, mirror neurons to biomimicry, homo mimeticus casts a shadow -- but also a light -- on the present and future, from social media to the Anthropocene." His subsequent edited volume *Homo Mimeticus II: Re-Turns to Mimesis* (Leuven University Press, 2024, with Marina Garcia-Granero) and special issue "The Mimetic Condition" (*CounterText* 8.1, 2022) provide the scholarly community extending these ideas.

**Nidesh Lawtoo, "(New) Fascism: Contagion, Community, Myth" (Michigan State UP, 2019)** -- Analyzes how authoritarian leaders exploit mimetic contagion through new media. For Cybroc, this work is relevant because Johnson's "Don't Die" movement operates through precisely the mimetic contagion Lawtoo describes -- followers imitate Johnson's protocols, spreading through social media.

**Vincenzo Idone Cassone, "Mimicking Gamers: Understanding Gamification Through Roger Caillois," *Games and Culture* 12(6), 2017**
Extends Caillois's mimicry concept to gamification, arguing that "the notion of mimicry helps in addressing the limits of definitions of gamification, as the act of making users mimic players, tied with the imitation of game elements and dynamics, blurs the distinction perceived between 'pure' game dynamics and non-gameful elements." Relevant because Cybroc's donation economy is essentially a gamified interaction where viewers "play at" supporting a performer.

### Literature Gap
No scholar has applied Caillois's "dangerous luxury" of mimicry to the recursive chain of wellness performance specifically. The broccoli-mimics-Johnson-mimics-nature chain represents a unique application that connects Caillois's 1935 insect theory to contemporary digital health culture. The paper can argue that Caillois's concept of mimicry as pathological self-dissolution is the precise mechanism by which biohacking culture operates -- the subject dissolves into protocol, as the insect dissolves into environment.

## 5. BAUDRILLARD'S SIMULACRA AND SIMULATION APPLIED TO WELLNESS/BIOHACKING

### Core Baudrillard Texts

**Jean Baudrillard, *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981; trans. Sheila Faria Glaser, University of Michigan Press, 1994)**
Baudrillard's four orders of the image: (1) faithful copy of reality, (2) distortion of reality, (3) masking the absence of reality, (4) pure simulacrum with no relation to reality. Johnson's Blueprint operates at the third-to-fourth order: it presents itself as optimal health but is actually a simulation constructed from biomarkers, supplements, and spectacle -- "masking the absence of a profound reality" (the impossibility of defeating death). The live-stream adds another simulacral layer: broccoli simulates Johnson who simulates "natural health" which simulates "nature" as sign-system -- a precession of simulacra with no originary reality.

**Jean Baudrillard, *The Consumer Society: Myths and Structures* (1970; trans. Chris Turner, Sage, 1998)**
Chapter 8, "The Finest Consumer Object: The Body," is directly relevant. Baudrillard argues the body has become "the finest consumer object" in consumer society -- both "capital and fetish." He writes: "one manages one's body; one handles it as one might handle an inheritance; one manipulates it as one of the many signifiers of social status." This is Johnson's Blueprint described *avant la lettre*: the body as investment portfolio, managed through obsessive disciplinary exercises for "aesthetic" ends. Baudrillard notes that "the cultural goals of beauty and elegance have translated into alibis for daily, obsessive disciplinary exercises on the body." Cybroc literalizes this by imposing these disciplinary exercises on a vegetable, revealing what Baudrillard implies: the exercises are structurally independent of the organism performing them.

### Applications to Digital Health Culture

**"Hyperreal Patients: Digital Twins as Simulacra and Their Impact on Clinical Heuristics," in *Digitale Ethik* (Springer, 2022)**
Examines digital twins in precision medicine through Baudrillard's simulacrum theory, arguing that virtual patient models built from health data create hyperreal bodies that may become "more real" than the physical patients they model. Directly parallel to how Johnson's biomarker data becomes more real than his lived body -- the quantified self precedes and produces the "reality" it claims to measure.

**Lucas Freund, "Beyond the Physical Self: Understanding the Perversion of Reality and the Desire for Digital Transcendence via Digital Avatars in the Context of Baudrillard's Theory," *AI & Society* (Springer, 2024)**
Explores the blurred distinction between real and simulated selves through Baudrillard's hyperreality, arguing that digital identity formation creates "a closed loop of hyperreality." For Cybroc, this closed loop maps onto the broccoli's situation: a simulated wellness subject performing simulated health protocols in a simulated care relationship with its audience.

**Ritzer, G. and Jurgenson, N., "Production, Consumption, Prosumption: The Nature of Capitalism in the Age of the Digital 'Prosumer,'" *Journal of Consumer Culture* 10(1), 2010**
While not specifically Baudrillardian, this article on prosumption illuminates how live-stream viewers simultaneously consume content and produce economic value through donations -- collapsing the production/consumption binary that Baudrillard identifies as a feature of late capitalism.

**Sanders, R., "Four Ironies of Self-Quantification: Wearable Technologies and the Quantified Self," *Science and Engineering Ethics* 27, 2020**
Identifies four ironies in self-tracking: "instrumental problems in human factors design" extending to "much broader social problems such as loss of freedom." Provides empirical grounding for the Baudrillardian argument that quantification replaces the body it claims to measure.

### Literature Gap (Critical)
**No existing scholarship directly applies Baudrillard to biohacking or wellness culture.** This is perhaps the most significant theoretical gap the paper can fill. The application is obvious once stated -- Johnson's entire project is a hyperreal simulation of health, where biomarkers (signs) replace the body (referent) -- but no scholar has made this argument in a peer-reviewed context. The Cybroc paper can pioneer this application.

## 6. ADDITIONAL CROSS-CUTTING REFERENCES

### Food/Dining in Art Practice

**Key scholarly texts:**
- Silvia Bottinelli and Margherita d'Ayala Valva, eds., *The Taste of Art: Food, Cooking, and Counterculture in Contemporary Practices* (University of Arkansas Press, 2017). Comprehensive scholarly collection.
- "Cooking and Dining as Forms of Public Art," *Food, Culture & Society* 24(4), 2021. Examines how artists transform dining into public art.
- "Feast: Radical Hospitality in Contemporary Art" (Smart Museum, University of Chicago, 2012). Exhibition presenting 30+ artists transforming shared meals into art.
- Plakias, A., "The Aesthetics of Food," *Philosophy Compass* 16(12), 2021. Surveys current debates in food aesthetics, noting Kant dismissed gustatory pleasure as lacking "disinterestedness" while Hegel excluded food from art because it "perishes in consumption." These philosophical exclusions make Cybroc's use of ephemeral Kaiseki as art-output theoretically provocative.

### Kaiseki as Aesthetic/Philosophical Practice

Kaiseki originates from Zen Buddhist *chakaiseki* (tea ceremony meal), from the practice of monks warming a stone (*kai*) against their stomachs (*seki*) to stave off hunger during meditation. Its philosophical foundations embrace *wabi-sabi* (beauty in imperfection/impermanence) and Zen mindfulness, emphasizing *shun* (seasonal peak), *omotenashi* (hospitality), and material simplicity. Each course is a temporary composition designed to be consumed -- the dish vanishes in the act of appreciation.

For Cybroc, the choice of Kaiseki is conceptually precise: it represents the intersection of aesthetic refinement and material impermanence that the entire installation theorizes. Using donation money earned by decaying broccoli performing longevity exercises to fund a meal tradition rooted in monks' hunger creates a multilayered irony connecting optimization culture, Buddhist impermanence, and consumptive luxury.

### The Gift Economy in Art Contexts

**Marcel Mauss, *The Gift: Forms and Functions of Exchange in Archaic Societies* (1925; trans. W.D. Halls, Routledge, 1990)** -- The three obligations: to give, to receive, to reciprocate. Cybroc systematically disrupts all three.

**Lewis Hyde, *The Gift: Creativity and the Artist in the Modern World* (Vintage, 1983/2007)** -- Distinguishes gift economy (art, creativity, reciprocal generosity) from commodity exchange (capitalism). Hyde argues artistic creation belongs fundamentally to the gift economy. Cybroc deliberately confuses these registers: the donation appears as gift but operates as commodity; the Kaiseki appears as luxury commodity but functions within the artwork's gift-logic.

**Bataille's potlatch connection** -- As noted above, Bataille's general economy theory reframes the Kaiseki dinner as potlatch: conspicuous expenditure of surplus that produces social prestige rather than durable goods. The Situationist International named their bulletin *Potlatch* precisely because the free distribution of art and ideas represented an alternative to commodity exchange.

## WHERE CYBROC MAKES A NOVEL CONTRIBUTION (Dimension 4 Specific)

1. **No existing artwork creates the specific closed-loop economic circuit** of live organism performing > earning real donations > funding ephemeral luxury dining. The self-consuming economic loop has no established art-theoretical framework; the paper can create one, drawing on Bataille's general economy, Bishop's critique of relational aesthetics, and Fraser's institutional self-reflexivity.

2. **No scholarship applies Baudrillard to biohacking or wellness culture.** The Consumer Society's "body as finest consumer object" chapter anticipates Johnson's Blueprint by 50 years, yet no scholar has made this connection explicit.

3. **No scholar has applied Caillois's "dangerous luxury" of mimicry** to the recursive chain of wellness performance. The broccoli-mimics-Johnson-mimics-nature chain is a genuinely original theoretical contribution.

4. **The intersection of parasocial relationship theory with non-sentient performers** (broccoli) has not been explored. Existing parasocial scholarship assumes at minimum a human or human-like performer capable of simulated reciprocity.

5. **The connection between Chinese live-streaming gifting culture and art-economic critique** represents an underexplored cross-cultural dimension. Given the authors' backgrounds (China Academy of Art, Tsinghua), this is a distinctive contribution.
