# Deep research framework for Cybroc: a bio-art critique of techno-immortalism

The theoretical landscape surrounding Cybroc is remarkably rich and, crucially, **largely uncharted at the specific intersections your work occupies**. No peer-reviewed scholarship yet analyzes Bryan Johnson as a cultural phenomenon; no existing bio-art work stages the collision between Silicon Valley longevity optimization and biological decay within an Artificial Life conference context; and no artwork has used a live-stream donation economy to fund its own ephemeral consumption as an explicit closed-loop commentary. This report maps the key references, arguments, and theoretical connections across all four requested dimensions, identifies comparable artworks for positioning, and flags the novel contributions Cybroc can claim.

## 1. Amusing ourselves to not-die: spectacle, transhumanism, and techno-faith

### Postman beyond television

Neil Postman's *Amusing Ourselves to Death* (1985) argues that television's epistemology transforms all public discourse into entertainment—oppression comes not from censorship but from addiction to amusement. His key concepts—**"the medium is the metaphor,"** the information-action ratio (where decontextualized information becomes disinformation), and the "now... this" erasure of coherence—apply with even greater force to social media platforms where Johnson's Blueprint circulates as content. Lance Strate's *Amazing Ourselves to Death* (Peter Lang, 2014), written by Postman's doctoral student, updates the thesis for Web 2.0, arguing that social media continues the "telegraphic discourse" bias toward novelty and imagery over substance. Jason Hannan's "Trolling Ourselves to Death?" (*European Journal of Communication* 33(2), 2018) extends the framework further, arguing we are now "trolling ourselves to death" through post-truth media ecologies. **The gap:** almost no scholars have applied Postman to wellness or health culture specifically—most applications concern politics and journalism. Extending his framework to biohacking culture is a novel move.

### Bryan Johnson as cultural phenomenon

Johnson (b. 1977), who sold Braintree to PayPal for **$800 million** in 2013, launched Project Blueprint in October 2021. He reportedly spends $2M+/year on anti-aging, employs 30+ doctors, tracks 1,000+ biomarkers, follows a strict 1,977-calorie vegan diet, and takes 100+ supplements daily. He experimented with plasma transfusions from his son (discontinued). His *Don't Die* book (2024) and the Netflix documentary *Don't Die: The Man Who Wants to Live Forever* (dir. Chris Smith) have amplified his cultural presence enormously. Johnson has declared "Don't Die is the next major ideology for the human race on par with democracy, capitalism, socialism, Islam, Christianity" (*TIME*, 2025).

Critiques remain primarily journalistic. The American Council on Science and Health (2025) noted his results constitute "the lowest level of scientific evidence"—anecdotal, with no way to isolate which interventions work. Harvard's Dr. Vadim Gladyshev stated Johnson's experiments have "almost no contribution to science." Dr. Cyriac Abby Philips compared him to Elizabeth Holmes. **Critically, there is no peer-reviewed academic analysis of Bryan Johnson as a cultural phenomenon.** This is the single most significant literature gap your paper can address.

### Transhumanism: key thinkers and critiques

The philosophical architecture of the "Don't Die" movement descends from formal transhumanism. Key reference points include Aubrey de Grey's concept of **"longevity escape velocity"** (coined with David Gobel; formalized in "Escape Velocity: Why the Prospect of Extreme Human Life Extension Matters Now," *PLoS Biology* 2(6), 2004)—the hypothetical point where medical advances extend life faster than time passes. Ray Kurzweil predicted this would arrive around 2029–2035. Michael Hauskeller's *Mythologies of Transhumanism* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2016) provides perhaps the strongest analytical framework, examining transhumanism as operating through **mythological narrative structures** rather than pure scientific reasoning—the broccoli cyborgs literalize this mythological absurdity. Melinda Hall's *The Bioethics of Enhancement* (Lexington Books, 2017) uses Foucault's "vile sovereignty" to argue transhumanism "rejects the body—especially the disabled body" and poses the question of which lives are worth living. McNamee and Edwards' "Transhumanism, Medical Technology and Slippery Slopes" (*Journal of Medical Ethics* 32(9), 2006) critiques transhumanism as "a quasi-medical ideology that seeks to evangelise its human-enhancing aims."

### Wellness as secular religion

Lena Rifai's "Whose Techno-Utopia?" (*Religion Compass* 16(12), 2022) is **the single most relevant article** for this sub-dimension. She analyzes biohacking as "committed to providing a form of this-worldly salvation to its largely white, male, and wealthy practitioners," arguing the movement perpetuates "Protestant privilege by universalizing Protestant ways of inhabiting the body." David Noble's *The Religion of Technology* (Penguin, 1997) traces a thousand-year Western tradition of religious transcendence driving technological development—the "Don't Die" philosophy as the latest iteration of millenarian techno-theology. Albert Antosca's "Technological Re-Enchantment" (*Humanities and Technology Review* 38(2), 2019) analyzes transhumanism as producing "post-secular techno-theology" where the Singularity functions as eschatological event.

### Debord, Han, and the commodification of mortality

Guy Debord's *Society of the Spectacle* (1967) traces "the decline of being into having, and having into merely appearing"—a trajectory that maps perfectly onto health → health metrics → health content → health commodity. Byung-Chul Han's *The Burnout Society* (Stanford UP, 2015) provides the sharpest contemporary framework: citizens are no longer "obedience-subjects" but **"achievement-subjects"—entrepreneurs of themselves** who practice "auto-exploitation" that "is more efficient than allo-exploitation, for the feeling of freedom attends it." Han specifically identifies "fitness zombies" as exemplary achievement-subjects. Johnson has literally surrendered autonomy to an algorithm—the ultimate achievement-subject whose "free constraint of maximizing achievement" maps directly onto his regimen. Joseph Reagle's *Hacking Life* (MIT Press, 2019) examines life hacking as "self-help for the digital age's creative class," noting that "compulsively checking your vital signs is its own sort of illness." Published by MIT Press, it provides a directly applicable cultural studies framework.

## 2. Performing decay: bio-art, ALife, and the aesthetics of rot

### The bio-art lineage

Cybroc inherits from a well-documented tradition. Yetisen et al.'s "Bioart" (*Trends in Biotechnology* 33(12), 2015) provides the most comprehensive peer-reviewed survey, tracing the field from Edward Steichen's colchicine-treated delphiniums through Joe Davis's *Microvenus* (1990, considered the first bio-art work), Eduardo Kac's GFP Bunny (2000), to SymbioticA's *Victimless Leather*. Kac's edited anthology *Signs of Life: Bio Art and Beyond* (MIT Press, 2007) establishes the ethical and conceptual frameworks, with Eugene Thacker on biomedia theory and Catts & Zurr on tissue culture art. Beatriz da Costa and Kavita Philip's *Tactical Biopolitics* (MIT Press, 2008) positions bio-art within Foucauldian biopolitics—art as intervention in biotechnological regimes. Cybroc extends this tradition by using a **commonplace grocery vegetable** rather than engineered cells or transgenic organisms—a democratized, absurdist bio-art gesture that makes the bio-art critique accessible rather than esoteric.

### Decay as medium: the vanitas tradition updated

Several major artworks foreground organic decomposition. **Dieter Roth** (1930–1998) is the primary precedent, deliberately using chocolate, cheese, sausage, and animal droppings as materials that transformed through exhibition. A Tate Papers scholarly essay addresses the museum's paradox: "the contradictory need to conserve that which was intended to be ephemeral." **Sam Taylor-Johnson's *Still Life*** (2001) compresses fruit decomposition into a 3-minute 44-second time-lapse, explicitly referencing 16th–17th century Dutch vanitas painting—a plastic Bic pen remains unchanged throughout, a quiet environmental commentary on what synthetic materials outlast organic life. **Jana Sterbak's *Vanitas: Flesh Dress for an Albino Anorexic*** (1987) displayed a dress of raw flank steak that slowly desiccated, connecting mortality, consumption, and fashion. Additional examples include Adrián Villar Rojas's *Theater of Disappearance* (2017, MOCA Geffen) with raw meat and fruit rotting in glass vitrines, Anya Gallaccio's flower and chocolate works, and Damien Hirst's *The Physical Impossibility of Death in the Mind of Someone Living* (1991), whose original shark ironically began decaying despite its preservation—unintentionally enacting the impossibility named in its title.

The vanitas tradition (skulls, wilting flowers, rotting fruit, hourglasses) provides Cybroc with a 400-year art-historical genealogy. Cybroc updates this as what might be called a **"techno-vanitas"**—a memento mori for the Silicon Valley optimization age, where broccoli cyborgs performing longevity exercises while inevitably decaying constitute a contemporary still life with algorithms.

### Artificial Life art and the life/death tension

Greenfield and Cao's "The Edge of Life-as-We-Know-It: Aesthetics of Decay within Artificial Life and Art" (*Technoetic Arts* 19(1–2), 2021) is **the single most directly relevant article for the Cybroc paper across all dimensions**. It directly addresses the intersection of decay aesthetics and ALife art, arguing that ALife research has privileged emergence, growth, and self-organization while **largely avoiding decay and death**. The authors identify a "decay taboo" in both Western culture and ALife, then reframe decay as "a constructive process of transformation." They reference Peter Todd's "Artificial Death" (ECAL 1993 proceedings) as an early exception. This article provides the exact theoretical scaffolding for arguing that Cybroc inserts "death-as-we-know-it" into the ALife conversation.

The *Meta-Life* anthology (Leonardo/MIT Press e-book, eds. Bureaud, Malina & Whiteley) collects 45+ articles from Leonardo documenting ALife art, including Edward Shanken's distinction between "life-as-we-know-it" and "life-as-it-could-be" (from Christopher Langton's foundational framework). Cybroc inverts this by staging "death-as-we-know-it" within a "life-as-it-could-be" research community. Sommerer and Mignonneau's *The Artwork as a Living System* (Leonardo Book Series, MIT Press, 2022) covers their pioneering *Interactive Plant Growing* (1992)—which used real plants as interfaces to grow virtual flora—creating an instructive contrast: their virtual plants grow indefinitely in computational space while Cybroc's real broccoli decays irreversibly.

N. Katherine Hayles' "Narratives of Artificial Life" (in *FutureNatural*, Routledge, 1996) provides philosophical depth, critiquing ALife's privileging of form over matter—Langton's claim that "aliveness will be found to be a property of logical form, not of material basis" enacts a Platonic erasure of the body and its decay. **Cybroc reasserts the material body against ALife's dematerializing tendencies.** The broccoli cannot be uploaded; its decay is irreducibly physical.

### Japanese aesthetics as philosophical counterpoint

Exhibiting in Kyoto—the historical center of tea ceremony aesthetics and wabi-sabi—amplifies Cybroc's resonances profoundly. Graham Parkes' "Japanese Aesthetics" (*Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*, 2023 revision) provides authoritative treatment of **mono no aware** (pathos of things), **wabi-sabi** (beauty in imperfection and impermanence), and **mujō** (Buddhist impermanence). Leonard Koren's *Wabi-Sabi: for Artists, Designers, Poets & Philosophers* (Imperfect Publishing, 1994/2008) defines wabi-sabi as "the beauty of things imperfect, impermanent, and incomplete" and explicitly positions it as "the antithesis of our classical Western notion of beauty as something perfect, enduring, and monumental." Its roots lie in Buddhism's three marks of existence: impermanence (*mujō*), suffering (*ku*), emptiness (*kū*).

The paper's strongest cross-cultural argument: **wabi-sabi and Western transhumanism represent diametrically opposed responses to mortality.** Johnson's Blueprint embodies the Western pursuit of perfection, endurance, and monumentality. The broccoli's three-day decay arc, exhibited in the historical center of impermanence aesthetics, performs mujō against the grain of transhumanist longevity. Impermanence wins. Kenkō's *Essays in Idleness* captures this: "Branches about to blossom or gardens strewn with faded flowers are worthier of our admiration."

## 3. Who gets to not die: ethics, biopolitics, and the optimized organism

### The justice problem of radical life extension

The bioethical literature on life extension centers on distributive justice. Michael Blake's "Life Extension and Civic Virtue" (*Journal of Ethics*, AMA, 2025) argues that radical life extension in an already unjust society would erode civic virtue—when the wealthy expect significantly longer lifespans, they become "prone to see the [poor] as fundamentally alien sorts of creatures." Francis Fukuyama's *Our Posthuman Future* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2002) warns biotechnology threatens liberal democracy's foundation in natural human equality, defining human dignity as grounded in what makes humans "more than the sum of their parts." Leon Kass's "wisdom of repugnance" (*The New Republic*, 1997; expanded in *Life, Liberty, and the Defense of Dignity*, 2002) argues visceral disgust at biotechnological interventions constitutes "the emotional expression of deep wisdom, beyond reason's power fully to articulate it." Cybroc complicates Kass's framework: the decaying broccoli performing longevity exercises provokes disgust, but **is the repugnance directed at the cyborg, or at the optimization culture it mirrors?**

### Living versus being optimized

Three philosophical traditions illuminate the distinction between genuine living and protocolized existence. **Heidegger's** *Sein-zum-Tode* (being-toward-death) from *Being and Time* (1927) holds that authentic existence requires confrontation with one's own finitude—"anticipatory resoluteness" toward death distinguishes genuine Dasein from inauthentic "falling." Johnson's "Don't Die" framing is the precise negation of authentic being-toward-death. The broccoli's three-day decay stages what Heidegger calls *Vorlaufen zum Tode*—running ahead toward death.

**Ivan Illich's** *Medical Nemesis* (1976) identifies three forms of iatrogenesis: clinical, social (where "life becomes a pilgrimage through check-ups and clinics"), and **cultural**—the destruction of traditional ways of dealing with death, pain, and sickness. His key insight: "Today's major pathogen... [is] the pursuit of the healthy body." A 2024 retrospective in the *British Journal of General Practice* confirmed his continued relevance. Johnson's Blueprint is the apotheosis of what Illich predicted—life entirely reduced to medical management.

**Nikolas Rose's** *The Politics of Life Itself* (Princeton UP, 2007) updates Foucault for the molecular age, introducing **"ethopolitics"**—self-governance through biological management—and "biological citizenship" where personhood is defined at the molecular level. Johnson's biomarker-tracked, supplement-optimized existence is a paradigmatic instance. The broccoli cyborg performing these protocols makes literal the concept of somatic individuality pushed to its absurd limit: what is the difference between Johnson's self-management and the mechanical manipulation of a vegetable?

### Disability studies strikes at the heart

This may be the most powerful critical lens for the paper. Alison Kafer's *Feminist, Queer, Crip* (Indiana UP, 2013) develops the **"curative imaginary"**—"an understanding of disability that not only expects and assumes intervention but also cannot imagine or comprehend anything other than intervention." Johnson's "Don't Die" extends the curative imaginary to all human embodiment: aging itself becomes the disability to be "cured." Kafer's concept of **"crip time"**—"time not just expanded but exploded"—offers the counterpoint: the broccoli's decay is not a failure to be cured but the natural unfolding of vegetal time, refusing the optimization timeline.

Rosemarie Garland-Thomson's concept of **"misfitting"** (*Hypatia* 26(3), 2011) describes the shifting relationship between embodied particularity and environment, arguing "mortality is the fundamental misfit of the human condition." The decaying broccoli cyborg is the ultimate misfit—an organism mechanically enrolled in protocols designed for human bodies, in environments built for optimization. Gregor Wolbring's "The Politics of Ableism" (*Development* 51(2), 2008) identifies a **"transhumanized version of ableism"** creating the "techno-poor disabled"—people who, by lacking enhancements, become effectively disabled. When optimization becomes mandatory, everything that ages or decays is by definition failing.

### Plant ethics and vegetal thinking

Michael Marder's *Plant-Thinking: A Philosophy of Vegetal Life* (Columbia UP, 2013) argues plants possess unique temporality, freedom, and "material knowledge or wisdom," occupying a "subaltern position" in Western philosophy. The mechanical imposition of human exercise protocols on vegetal matter is precisely the **"logic of totalization"** Marder warns against—treating plant life as instrumental, failing to attend to its own mode of being. The broccoli's decay becomes its own form of plant-thinking: non-cognitive resistance to imposed optimization.

Nora Vaage's "What Ethics for Bioart?" (*NanoEthics* 10(1), 2016) examines how bio-artists must navigate ethics committees that are often "at a loss" when dealing with art projects using living organisms. Cybroc introduces a novel ethical dimension: working with organisms already "condemned" to consumption, reframing their prior instrumentalization through satirical protocols that mirror the optimization culture's instrumentalization of human bodies.

### When does satire become complicity?

Claire Bishop's *Artificial Hells* (Verso, 2012) argues art must maintain "a degree of autonomy and unreadability in order to resist co-option"—art should not be immune from aesthetic criticism merely because it has good intentions. Jacques Rancière's *The Politics of Aesthetics* (Continuum, 2004) introduces the **"distribution of the sensible"**—the system determining what is visible and thinkable. Art's political power lies in reconfiguring this distribution. Cybroc performs what Rancière would call **"dissensus"** by introducing the decaying vegetal body as a new political subject into optimization discourse—disrupting the assumed partition between organisms that "deserve" enhancement and those that don't.

## 4. The closed loop: live-streaming, donation economy, and recursive mimicry

### Attention merchants and parasocial vegetables

Tim Wu's *The Attention Merchants* (Knopf, 2016) traces the commodification of attention from penny newspapers to digital platforms, conceptualizing media companies as "attention brokers" operating between markets with different currencies—money on one side, attention on the other. Cybroc's broccoli cyborgs literalize this: vegetable performers become attention merchants, harvesting viewer gaze and converting it into donations. The absurdity of the performer exposes what Wu's framework implies—**the attention economy is indifferent to the content of what captures attention.**

Johnson and Woodcock's "'And Today's Top Donator is': How Live Streamers on Twitch.tv Monetize and Gamify Their Broadcasts" (*Social Media + Society* 5(4), 2019) presents a seven-part typology of streaming monetization, finding that donation cultures operate as *do ut des* exchanges—viewers donate for recognition and parasocial intimacy. In Cybroc, this logic is hilariously inverted: viewers donate to broccoli—a nonhuman, non-sentient performer incapable of reciprocating recognition. Jonathan Crary's *24/7: Late Capitalism and the End of Sleep* (Verso, 2013) argues capitalism demands continuous availability; Johnson's Blueprint epitomizes Crary's 24/7 subject, and the live-stream format extends this logic to the broccoli that never sleeps.

### The self-consuming economic circuit

The installation's economic architecture—broccoli performs → earns donations → donations fund Kaiseki dinner → dinner is consumed and vanishes—demands analysis through relational aesthetics and its critics. Nicolas Bourriaud's *Relational Aesthetics* (Les Presses du Réel, 1998/2002) defines art as creating social environments for shared activities and "interstices" that "elude the capitalist economic context." The Kaiseki dinner is a relational artwork—a shared meal directly output by the performance circuit. But unlike Rirkrit Tiravanija's paradigmatic *Untitled (Free)* (1992), where Thai curry was served freely to strangers, **Cybroc's meal is funded by others and consumed exclusively by the artists.** This inversion is crucial.

Claire Bishop's "Antagonism and Relational Aesthetics" (*October* 110, 2004) charges relational art with "collapsing into compensatory entertainment" and ignoring institutional complicity with capitalism. Stewart Martin's "Critique of Relational Aesthetics" (*Third Text* 21(4), 2007) argues Bourriaud's theory is "helplessly reversible into an aestheticisation of capitalist exchange." Cybroc refuses the pretense of being free from capitalist exchange—**the donation-to-dinner pipeline is the artwork's stated content, not its concealed economic infrastructure.** This is honest staging rather than mystification.

The choice of Kaiseki is conceptually rich. Japanese multi-course haute cuisine, rooted in Zen Buddhist temple cooking, is itself a form of aesthetic minimalism—each course a temporary composition designed to be consumed. Using donation money to fund ephemeral luxury consumption creates a loop where the artwork's economic product vanishes in the act of consumption—like Klein throwing gold into the Seine, but with calories.

### Art as economic system: comparable works

Several precedents contextualize Cybroc's economic dimension. **Santiago Sierra** pays marginalized workers to perform degrading tasks within gallery settings, collapsing the distance between art and wage labor. Where Sierra makes human exploitation visible by replicating it, Cybroc substitutes broccoli for the exploited laborer—if viewers will pay to watch vegetables exercise, what does this say about the attention economy's indifference to the content of labor? **Yves Klein's *Zone de Sensibilité Picturale Immatérielle*** (1959–62) sold receipts for empty space in exchange for gold; buyers completed the purchase by burning the receipt while Klein threw half the gold into the Seine. Both works create circuits culminating in the destruction of value—Klein's gold dissolves into the Seine, Cybroc's donation money dissolves into a meal. **Caleb Larsen's *A Tool to Deceive and Slaughter*** (2009), a black box perpetually self-auctioning on eBay, is an artwork-as-economic-engine with the opposite temporal structure: perpetual circulation without consumption, versus Cybroc's circuit that terminates in consumption.

### Caillois and the recursive mimicry chain

Roger Caillois provides two essential frameworks. In *Man, Play and Games* (1961), mimicry is one of four play categories involving "making oneself or others believe that one is different from oneself." Cybroc operates across all four simultaneously: the broccoli's mimicry of exercise (mimicry), the audience's unpredictable donations (alea), the implied competition with Johnson's metrics (agôn), and the overall vertiginous absurdity (ilinx).

More powerfully, Caillois's "Mimicry and Legendary Psychasthenia" (*Minotaure* 7, 1935; reprinted *October* 31, 1984) argues that insect mimicry is not merely adaptive but represents a **"dangerous luxury"—an excess revealing a drive toward dissolution of the self into environment.** The organism "no longer knows where to place itself." The recursive mimicry chain in Cybroc creates precisely this dissolution: broccoli mimics Johnson → Johnson mimics "natural health" → "natural health" is already a cultural construct → each layer dissolves the distinction between model and copy. The broccoli performing human exercises is a literalization of Caillois's dangerous luxury—mimicry in excess of any survival function, performed by an organism that cannot benefit from the imitation. Nidesh Lawtoo's *Homo Mimeticus* project (2022–present) extends Caillois to the Anthropocene, theorizing mimicry as "a material, affective concept beyond aesthetic realism."

### Baudrillard's precession of simulacra

Jean Baudrillard's *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981; trans. 1994) describes four orders of the image, from faithful copy to pure simulacrum with no relation to reality. Johnson's Blueprint operates at the third-to-fourth order: it presents itself as optimal health but is actually **a simulation constructed from biomarkers, supplements, and spectacle—"masking the absence of a profound reality"** (the impossibility of defeating death). The live-stream adds another simulacral layer: broccoli simulates Johnson who simulates "natural health" which simulates "nature" as sign-system—a precession of simulacra with no originary reality. Johnson's biomarker data becomes more real than the lived body; the quantified self precedes and produces the "reality" it claims to measure. **No existing scholarship directly applies Baudrillard to biohacking or wellness culture**—this is another gap the paper can fill.

## Comparable artworks for positioning Cybroc

The following works share significant conceptual territory with Cybroc and should be explicitly addressed for positioning:

**Most directly comparable (address in detail):** Oron Catts & Ionat Zurr's *Victimless Leather* (2004)—a semi-living jacket grown from cells that died when life-support was disconnected. Like Cybroc, the death of the biological material is integral to the work's meaning. However, Cybroc uses common produce rather than engineered tissue, and its satirical target (longevity culture) differs fundamentally from TC&A's investigation of biotechnological ethics.

**Decay-art precedents:** Dieter Roth's food decomposition works (1960s–90s), Sam Taylor-Johnson's *Still Life* (2001), Jana Sterbak's *Vanitas: Flesh Dress* (1987), Adrián Villar Rojas's *Theater of Disappearance* (2017). These share the decay-as-medium approach but none target techno-immortalism or operate within an ALife conference context.

**Economic circuit artworks:** Rirkrit Tiravanija's cooking works (relational meals, but free and egalitarian—Cybroc's dinner is funded by others and exclusively consumed by artists), Santiago Sierra (labor exploitation as medium), Yves Klein's *Zone de Sensibilité Picturale Immatérielle* (self-negating transaction), Caleb Larsen's *A Tool to Deceive and Slaughter* (artwork-as-economic-engine).

**ALife art:** Sommerer & Mignonneau's *Interactive Plant Growing* (1992) used real plants to grow virtual flora—digital immortality versus biological mortality. Theo Jansen's *Strandbeests* aspire to autonomous survival, paralleling longevity aspirations.

**No existing artwork combines all of Cybroc's elements:** bio-art decay + longevity culture critique + live-stream donation economy + ALife conference context + Japanese impermanence aesthetics. This combination is genuinely novel.

## Where Cybroc makes a novel contribution

The literature gaps identified across all four dimensions suggest **five specific novelty claims** for the paper's contribution statement:

**First**, Cybroc would be among the first peer-reviewed academic analyses of Bryan Johnson and the "Don't Die" movement as a cultural phenomenon. Most existing critique is journalistic; no bioethics or cultural studies journal article specifically examines Johnson's project.

**Second**, the paper can pioneer the application of Postman's media ecology, Han's achievement society, and Debord's spectacle theory to wellness and longevity optimization culture. These theorists have been applied extensively to politics and media but almost never to biohacking or health optimization.

**Third**, Greenfield and Cao (2021) identified that ALife research privileges emergence while avoiding decay. Cybroc directly confronts this avoidance by staging biological decay within an ALife conference, making what they call "the edge of life-as-we-know-it" the artwork's central material. The paper extends their theoretical framework into artistic practice.

**Fourth**, the concept of **"vegetal biopolitics"** is genuinely new—revealing that optimization culture's logic, when applied across ontological boundaries (human to plant), exposes its fundamental emptiness. The broccoli cannot "want" to not die, yet the protocols proceed, revealing they were never about the organism's desires.
