### 1. Bioengineered Living Entities in Art: Aliveness, Duration, and Movement in Bricolage

**Authors:** Ziggy O'Reilly, Christina Chau, Nathan Thompson, Guy Ben-Ary
**Citation:** Leonardo 56(5): 496--500, 2023
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1162/leon_a_02402
**Section:** General Articles

**Summary:** This paper documents *Bricolage*, a kinetic biological artwork first exhibited at the Perth International Arts Festival in 2020. The artists used stem cell technologies to create bioengineered living entities from donated human heart muscle cells. These living entities are suspended in an incubator visible to gallery-goers, who watch the "performance" of cells generating movement and contracting independently. The paper considers how the assemblage, animation, and performance of cells highlight questions around conceptualizations and perceptions of life, duration, animation, and aliveness.

**Why relevant to Cybroc:** This is one of the closest parallels in Leonardo. Both works feature biological entities "performing" in a gallery/exhibition context. Both address temporality and duration of living materials. Both raise questions about what constitutes life, movement, and agency. The Cybroc broccoli perform exercises and decay; the heart cells perform contractions and eventually cease. Both use the language of performance to describe biological processes.

**Writing style:** Third-person scholarly with first-person practitioner reflections. Hybrid academic-artistic voice. Includes discussion of the artists' intentions alongside theoretical framing.

### 2. Epiphytic Memory: A Cognitive Assemblage of Plant-Human-Technology

**Authors:** Finn Petrie
**Citation:** Leonardo 56(5): 478--484, 2023
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1162/leon_a_02367
**Section:** Artists' Articles

**Summary:** An ongoing project motivated by symbiotic homing relations of plants. The artist 3D-printed porcelain LIDAR scans of ancient trees from Aotearoa New Zealand's southern rainforests, situating them as site-specific sculptures that function both as memories and as potential bioscaffolds for new life. The project uses augmented reality to simulate plant growth and contextualizes the work through scientific research and Indigenous Maori thought on plant relations and intelligence, drawing on N. Katherine Hayles's ideas of planetary cognitive ecology.

**Why relevant to Cybroc:** Plant-based art practice with a strong theoretical framework. First-person artist writing about their own work. The project treats plants as agents with intelligence and memory, paralleling Cybroc's treatment of broccoli as performers. Both works sit at the intersection of plant science, technology, and art. Both address ecological themes through practice-based research.

**Writing style:** First-person artist narrative with theoretical interjections drawing on philosophy of mind and indigenous knowledge systems. Reflective, practice-led voice.

### 3. Growing Semi-Living Sculptures: The Tissue Culture & Art Project

**Authors:** Oron Catts and Ionat Zurr
**Citation:** Leonardo 35(4): 365--370, 2002
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1162/002409402760181123
**Section:** Artists' Articles

**Summary:** A foundational bio-art paper describing the Tissue Culture & Art (TC&A) Project. Over six years, the authors grew tissue sculptures -- "semi-living objects" -- by culturing cells on artificial scaffolds. They created tissue constructs of varying geometrical complexity and size to develop a new artistic palette that challenges perceptions regarding the utilization of new biological knowledge. The paper discusses the ethical implications, the "semi-living" category between living and dead, and the artists' process of working in biology labs.

**Why relevant to Cybroc:** A landmark precedent for bio-art in Leonardo's Artists' Articles section. Both works address the boundary between living and non-living, and both use biological decay and mortality as core artistic material. Catts and Zurr's "semi-living" concept resonates with the Cybroc's broccoli-machine hybrid. Both works are presented at the intersection of art and biological science. The ironic, critical stance toward biotechnology parallels Cybroc's satire of optimization culture.

**Writing style:** First-person plural (we/our) artist-researchers describing their practice. Combines practical descriptions of lab work with theoretical and ethical reflection. Direct, accessible prose.

### 5. Notes on Genetic Art

**Authors:** George Gessert
**Citation:** Leonardo 26(3): 205--211, 1993
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.2307/1575814
**Section:** Artists' Articles

**Summary:** A pioneering paper arguing that genetic manipulation of organisms -- particularly plant hybridization -- constitutes an unacknowledged form of art. Gessert discusses how domesticated ornamental plants, pets, and consciousness-altering drug plants constitute a vast genetic folk art. He describes his own work with Pacific Coast native irises, hybridizing wild varieties and selecting for aesthetic qualities, exploring how human aesthetic preferences affect evolution.

**Why relevant to Cybroc:** The foundational precedent for plant-based bio-art in Leonardo. Both papers treat plants as artistic medium and address the intersection of aesthetics and biological processes. Both are written in first-person by an artist reflecting on their practice. Gessert's critical perspective on how aesthetics drives genetic selection parallels Cybroc's critique of how optimization culture drives biological intervention. Both works challenge what counts as "art material."

**Writing style:** First-person essayistic. Reflective, personal voice. Combines art practice description with philosophical and historical analysis. Accessible, non-jargon-heavy prose.

## Top 5 Most Similar Papers: Extended Analysis

### 1st Most Similar: O'Reilly et al., "Bioengineered Living Entities in Art: Bricolage" (2023)

**Similarity score: Very High**

*Bricolage* is the single most similar published work to Cybroc in Leonardo. Key parallels:
- **Living biological material performing:** Heart cells contract and move in an incubator; broccoli cyborgs perform exercises and decay in an exhibition space. Both treat biological processes as "performances."
- **Duration and decay as theme:** Both works exist on a timeline of biological activity and eventual cessation. The heart cells' movement duration is a key concern; Cybroc's three-day decay timeline is central.
- **Exhibition context:** Both were shown at art festivals/conferences and required specific environmental conditions (incubator / exhibition hall temperature).
- **Questions about "aliveness":** Both papers explicitly address what constitutes life, animation, and the boundaries of the living.
- **Art-science methodology:** Both emerge from laboratory practices applied to artistic contexts.

**Key difference:** Bricolage uses human-derived cells, which raises different ethical stakes than using vegetables. Bricolage is more earnest/serious in tone, while Cybroc employs satire.

**Strategic lesson:** Cybroc should cite this paper as a direct precedent, noting both the parallel (biological performance) and the divergence (satirical vs. earnest framing; vegetable vs. human tissue).

### 2nd Most Similar: Catts & Zurr, "Growing Semi-Living Sculptures" (2002)

**Similarity score: Very High**

The Tissue Culture & Art Project represents the foundational bio-art practice in Leonardo's history. Key parallels:
- **"Semi-living" category:** Catts and Zurr coined the concept of the "semi-living" -- entities that are part living, part constructed. The Cybroc's broccoli cyborgs occupy exactly this category.
- **Artists' Articles section:** This is the paradigmatic example of an artist writing about bio-art practice in Leonardo. It established the template that Cybroc follows.
- **Irony and critique:** Catts and Zurr are known for using irony and conceptual provocation in their bio-art. Their "Disembodied Cuisine" project (growing and eating frog steaks) parallels Cybroc's use of food/edible organisms as artistic material.
- **Lab-as-studio practice:** Both works required working with biological materials in controlled conditions.

**Strategic lesson:** Cybroc can position itself within the lineage of Catts and Zurr's semi-living art, while noting that it uses a more accessible biological material (grocery-store broccoli) and a more explicitly satirical/performative framework.

## How These Papers Inform the Cybroc Submission Strategy

### 1. Section Targeting

The papers above appear across several Leonardo sections:
- **Artists' Articles / Work Bench:** The primary home for first-person artist accounts of practice (Petrie, Catts/Zurr, Kac, Gessert, Bergamo, Barker/Burd, Ballengee, O'Neill, Stelarc, Sommerer/Mignonneau). This is the strongest fit for the Cybroc paper given its first-person artist voice and practice-based content.
- **Work Bench (new name):** Berry-Frith and Sueyoshi/Morimoto demonstrate that the renamed section continues to accept art-science practice papers.
- **General Articles:** Some bio-art papers appear here (Bricolage, OOO/Bacterial Art), particularly when they include more theoretical or analytical framing alongside practice documentation.

**Recommendation:** Target the **Work Bench** section (formerly Artists' Articles). The Cybroc paper's first-person voice and practice-based content align perfectly with this section's mission of featuring "articles written by creative practitioners, offering intimate insights into individual cross-disciplinary processes."

### 2. Writing Style Precedents

The survey reveals several acceptable writing modes in Leonardo:
- **First-person artist narrative** (Kac, Gessert, Stelarc, O'Neill, Bergamo) -- the most common voice in Artists' Articles
- **First-person plural collaborative** (Catts/Zurr, Barker/Burd, Chua/Chester, Ballengee) -- for co-authored work
- **Dialogic/multi-voice** (McDonald/Panneels) -- the most relevant precedent for Cybroc's two-voice structure
- **Hybrid practice-theory** (Petrie, Berry-Frith, Saeki et al.) -- combining artistic practice with theoretical framing

**Recommendation:** The Cybroc paper's two-voice structure (artist narrative + critical interjections) is supported by the McDonald/Panneels precedent of "dialogic autoethnography." Frame the two-voice structure as a deliberate methodological choice -- a "dialogic performative" approach that enacts the work's themes within the writing itself.

### 4. Key Arguments for Novelty

While these papers provide precedent, the Cybroc paper offers several novel contributions that distinguish it:
- **Satirical voice in bio-art:** While Catts/Zurr use irony, and Kazakou uses humor, the Cybroc paper's explicit satire of longevity/optimization culture is unusual in Leonardo. This should be framed as a deliberate artistic and scholarly strategy.
- **Vegetables as performers:** No paper in the survey uses common food vegetables as protagonists of an art installation. This domesticity and ordinariness is itself a critical gesture.
- **ALife conference as art venue:** While Sommerer/Mignonneau and others have connections to ALife, presenting work *at* the ALIFE conference itself and documenting it in Leonardo creates a unique art-science bridge.
- **Three-day decay timeline:** The specific use of natural biological decay as a performative clock is distinctive. While Chua/Chester's melting ice is similar, the use of rotting food specifically invokes mortality, consumption, and waste in ways that ice does not.
- **Two-voice performative structure:** While McDonald/Panneels use dialogic format, the Cybroc paper's specific use of critical theoretical interjections disrupting an artist narrative is structurally distinct and enacts the work's themes of disruption and critique.
