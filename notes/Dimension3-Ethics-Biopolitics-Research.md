## 1. BIOETHICS OF RADICAL LIFE EXTENSION

### Bioconservative Critiques

**Francis Fukuyama, *Our Posthuman Future: Consequences of the Biotechnology Revolution*. New York: Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2002. ISBN: 978-0-374-23643-4.**
Fukuyama argues that biotechnology poses the most significant threat to liberal democracy since fascism and communism, not because of any single technology but because enhancement threatens the concept of a shared human nature -- what he calls "Factor X" -- on which liberal egalitarianism depends. If intrinsic differences between human beings are introduced via chemical or genetic means, the resulting stratification could produce "master and slave, gene-rich and gene-poor, suprahuman and subhuman" classes. He calls for national and international regulation to discriminate between technologies that promote human flourishing and those that threaten human dignity. **For Cybroc:** Fukuyama's "Factor X" argument is satirised by the installation -- Bryan Johnson's Blueprint protocol seeks to optimise a single human body beyond ordinary biological limits, but Cybroc extends this optimization logic to a vegetable, exposing the absurdity of treating biological "potential" as something to be maximised. If broccoli can be made to do push-ups, the species-boundary of "Factor X" is revealed as arbitrary.

**Leon Kass, *Life, Liberty and the Defense of Dignity: The Challenge for Bioethics*. San Francisco: Encounter Books, 2002. ISBN: 978-1-893554-55-9. Also: "The Wisdom of Repugnance." *The New Republic*, 2 June 1997.**
Kass coined the phrase "wisdom of repugnance" to argue that visceral disgust at biotechnological manipulation carries genuine moral insight -- "repugnance is the emotional expression of deep wisdom, beyond reason's power fully to articulate it." As chairman of the President's Council on Bioethics (2001--2005), Kass opposed human cloning, life extension, euthanasia, and embryo research. He challenges us to confront the posthuman future by thinking deeply about the life-and-death issues we face. **For Cybroc:** The installation weaponises repugnance -- audiences experience disgust at the rotting broccoli performing exercises, but this repugnance carries its own wisdom: it reveals the hubris of the anti-aging imperative. The broccoli doing push-ups provokes exactly the visceral reaction Kass theorises, yet here it cuts against optimisation rather than against biotechnology per se.

### Pro-Extension and Transhumanist Ethics

**John Harris, "Intimations of Immortality -- The Ethics and Justice of Life-Extending Therapies." *Current Legal Problems* 55(1): 65--97, 2002. Also: "Immortal Ethics." *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences* 1019: 527--534, 2004. DOI: 10.1196/annals.1297.098.**
Harris, Professor of Bioethics at the University of Manchester, argues that all biomedical scientists already aim to extend life, and that "when you save a life, you are simply postponing death to another point. Thus, we are committed to extending life indefinitely if we can, for the same reasons that we are committed to life-saving." He examines arguments against life extension and finds them wanting, arguing death is "merely an obstacle, a disability" whose goodness is greatly outweighed by its elimination. On distributive justice, Harris contends we are not morally obliged to deny life extension to all in order to avoid denying it to some -- rather, we should make interventions available "as justly and widely as possible." **For Cybroc:** Harris's framing of death as "disability" is precisely what the installation satirises. The broccoli, already "dead" in any meaningful sentient sense (harvested, severed from root), is nonetheless subjected to life-extending exercises -- exposing the category error in treating mortality as a mere technical problem.

**Nick Bostrom, "In Defence of Posthuman Dignity." *Bioethics* 19(3): 202--214, 2005. DOI: 10.1111/j.1467-8519.2005.00437.x. Also: "Transhumanist Values." *Journal of Philosophical Research* 30 (supplement): 3--14, 2005. Also: "Letter from Utopia." *Studies in Ethics, Law, and Technology* 2(1), 2008.**
Bostrom, founding director of the Future of Humanity Institute at Oxford, is the foremost philosophical advocate for transhumanism. He argues that positions on enhancement range from transhumanism to bioconservatism, and that "by defending posthuman dignity we promote a more inclusive and humane ethics, one that will embrace future technologically modified people." His "Letter from Utopia" is a fictionalized address from a possible transhuman future to present-day humans, urging them to pursue enhancement. Transhumanism holds that human nature is "improvable through the use of applied science and other rational methods, which may make it possible to increase human health-span, extend our intellectual and physical capacities, and give us increased control over our own mental states and moods." **For Cybroc:** Bostrom's utopian vision is deflated by the installation -- the broccoli cyborg is the absurd endpoint of enhancement logic, a "posthuman" vegetable whose "improved" state (performing exercises) cannot prevent its fundamental decay. The gap between Bostrom's aspirational rhetoric and the rotting reality of the broccoli performs the critique.

**Michael Blake, "Life Extension and Civic Virtue." *AMA Journal of Ethics* 27(12): E853--858, 2025.**
Blake, a philosopher at the University of Washington, argues that inequitable access to interventions capable of dramatically extending human lifespans would undermine individual and collective upkeep of civic virtue. He focuses on the impact of "longevity gaps" on the virtues of minimal altruism and moral motivation required for a citizen to participate in a liberal polity, arguing that life extension in societies characterised by widespread inequality in wealth and power would have a corrosive effect on democratic governance. **For Cybroc:** Blake's argument about longevity gaps is literalised by the installation's donation economy -- the broccoli's "exercise" is funded by viewer donations that ultimately pay for the artists' Kaiseki dinner. The gap between the broccoli's futile optimization and the artists' consumption mirrors the structural inequalities Blake identifies.

### Longevity Escape Velocity and the Anti-Aging Movement

**Aubrey de Grey and Michael Rae, *Ending Aging: The Rejuvenation Breakthroughs That Could Reverse Human Aging in Our Lifetime*. New York: St. Martin's Press, 2007. ISBN: 978-0-312-36706-0.**
De Grey, a biomedical gerontologist, proposes "Strategies for Engineered Negligible Senescence" (SENS) -- a detailed plan to prevent age-related decline through repairing seven categories of cellular and molecular damage. He coined the concept of "longevity escape velocity" (LEV): a hypothetical situation in which medical advances increase one's remaining life expectancy more than the time that passes. De Grey predicts humanity has a 50% chance of reaching LEV by the mid-to-late 2030s and claims that when achieved, it will be free. **For Cybroc:** De Grey's LEV is directly parodied -- the broccoli's decay is irreversible despite the exercise protocol. No amount of push-ups, squats, or stretches can achieve "escape velocity" for a severed vegetable. The three-day timeframe of the installation (ALIFE 2025, Kyoto) is a countdown that escape velocity cannot outrun.

**Bryan Johnson and the Blueprint Protocol.**
Johnson, an American entrepreneur and venture capitalist, initiated Project Blueprint in 2020 -- spending approximately $2 million per year to "reverse aging." The protocol involves 54+ daily supplements, a strictly plant-based diet (70 pounds of vegetables monthly), intermittent fasting (eating between 6 a.m. and 11 a.m.), precise sleep optimization (achieving and maintaining a "perfect" sleep score for months), and constant biometric tracking. Johnson claims to have reversed his biological age by 5.1 years and slowed his aging rate to 0.69 (aging eight months for every twelve calendar months). However, as the American Council on Science and Health notes, "this is anecdotal evidence or, at best, a case study, representing the lowest level of scientific evidence." Johnson's motto "Don't Die" has become a cultural touchstone of the biohacking movement. **For Cybroc:** The installation's entire conceit -- broccoli performing Johnson's exercise protocols -- directly satirises Blueprint's combination of extreme self-governance and performative self-documentation. The broccoli does everything Johnson does (exercises, is constantly monitored, is live-streamed) except the one thing that matters: it cannot avoid death. The vegetable's indifference to its own optimization is the installation's central joke and its deepest philosophical provocation.

### The Biohacking / Quantified Self Movement

**Deborah Lupton, *The Quantified Self: A Sociology of Self-Tracking*. Cambridge: Polity Press, 2016. ISBN: 978-1-5095-0059-8.**
Lupton provides the definitive sociological analysis of self-tracking culture. Drawing on Foucault, Latour, and critical data studies, she identifies five modes of self-tracking: private, communal, pushed, imposed, and exploited. She argues the Quantified Self conforms to the ideal neoliberal citizen -- "the self-optimizing individual who voluntarily monitors, measures, regulates and collects biometric data." Lupton critiques the "masculinist underpinnings of self-tracking technologies, particularly the distrust of bodily signals, feelings, and non-numerical information." Her concept of "dataveillance" -- the surveillance of individuals through their personal data -- shows how self-tracking data is repurposed for commercial, governmental, and research purposes. **For Cybroc:** The broccoli is the ultimate quantified self -- tracked, measured, live-streamed, yet generating data that describes only decay. The installation exposes self-tracking's fundamental assumption: that data equals control. The broccoli's data is meticulous but meaningless -- rot proceeds regardless of how precisely it is documented.

**Antti Lindfors, "Between Self-Tracking and Alternative Medicine: Biomimetic Imaginary in Contemporary Biohacking." *Body & Society* 30(1--2), 2024. DOI: 10.1177/1357034X231218413.**
Lindfors analyses biohacking as "a loose assembly of somatic and semiotic techniques of the self and an accompanied ethos of self-directed bodily transformation, at the intersection between data-driven self-tracking and complementary and alternative medicine." He interrogates biohacking's "ideological investments and complexities with contemporary cultural dominants including individualism, consumerism and neoliberalism." **For Cybroc:** The broccoli cyborg is a biomimetic parody -- it mimics the biohacker's self-directed bodily transformation, but the "body" being transformed is already in terminal decline.

## 2. BIOPOLITICS AND THE GOVERNANCE OF LIFE

### Foucault's Biopower and Biopolitics

**Michel Foucault, *The History of Sexuality, Volume 1: An Introduction* [*La volonte de savoir*, 1976]. Trans. Robert Hurley. New York: Vintage, 1978. ISBN: 978-0-394-74026-3.**
Part Five, "Right of Death and Power over Life," is the foundational statement of biopower. Foucault describes a historical shift from sovereign power (the right to "take life or let live") to biopower (the power to "foster life or disallow it to the point of death"). Biopower operates through two poles: an "anatomo-politics of the human body" (disciplinary power over individual bodies) and a "biopolitics of the population" (regulatory controls over species-level processes: birth rate, longevity, health). Foucault argues biopower was indispensable to the rise of capitalism, requiring "the controlled insertion of bodies into the machinery of production and the adjustment of the phenomena of population to economic processes." **For Cybroc:** Johnson's Blueprint is a textbook case of biopower internalised -- disciplinary power over the individual body (exercise protocols, supplement regimes) combined with population-level biopolitics (the movement aims to shift collective norms around aging). The broccoli, subjected to the same disciplinary apparatus, reveals what biopower looks like when stripped of consent and consciousness: pure governance of biological matter.

**Michel Foucault, *"Society Must Be Defended": Lectures at the College de France, 1975--1976*. Trans. David Macey. New York: Picador, 2003. ISBN: 978-0-312-42266-0.**
These lectures develop Foucault's theory of biopower in relation to state racism and the "war model" of politics. He argues that modern states exercise power through the management of populations -- making certain populations live while "disallowing" others to the point of death. The March 17, 1976 lecture contains the crucial formulation of biopower as a technology that addresses the population as a "species body." **For Cybroc:** The installation satirically extends biopolitics to the vegetal kingdom. The broccoli is governed as a "species body" -- subjected to protocols designed for a different species entirely, in a biopolitical logic that cannot recognise the absurdity of its own application.

**Michel Foucault, *The Birth of Biopolitics: Lectures at the College de France, 1978--1979*. Ed. Michel Senellart. Trans. Graham Burchell. New York: Palgrave Macmillan, 2008. ISBN: 978-1-4039-8654-2.**
These lectures analyse neoliberalism as a mode of governmentality, focusing on German ordoliberalism and the Chicago School. Foucault describes the transformation of *homo oeconomicus* from "partner of exchange" to "entrepreneur of himself" or "enterprise-unit" -- the individual who views themselves as a holder of "human capital" (both innate and acquired). The neoliberal subject is not merely disciplined but self-governing, optimising their own life as a form of capital investment. **For Cybroc:** Johnson is the paradigmatic *homo oeconomicus* as entrepreneur of himself -- investing $2 million annually in his own biological capital. The broccoli's forced exercises parody this self-entrepreneurship: it is made to "invest" in its bodily capital through exercises it cannot choose, benefit from, or refuse.

### Post-Foucauldian Biopolitics

**Nikolas Rose, "The Politics of Life Itself." *Theory, Culture & Society* 18(6): 1--30, 2001. DOI: 10.1177/02632760122052020. Expanded as: *The Politics of Life Itself: Biomedicine, Power, and Subjectivity in the Twenty-First Century*. Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2007. ISBN: 978-0-691-12191-8.**
Rose updates Foucault for the age of molecular biology, arguing that contemporary biopolitics operates at the molecular level -- not through disciplinary institutions but through "ethopolitics," "biological citizenship," and "somatic individuality." He identifies three dimensions of contemporary biopolitics: logics of control (risk politics), regimes of truth (molecular politics), and technologies of the self (ethopolitics). Under ethopolitics, individuals govern themselves through biological self-knowledge -- genetic testing, pharmacological self-modulation, lifestyle optimization. "Somatic individuality" describes how personhood is increasingly defined in terms of corporeality, with "new and direct relations established between our biology and our conduct." Rose argues that a threshold has been crossed into a world of "biological citizenship" in which humans view themselves at the molecular level. **For Cybroc:** Rose's "somatic individuality" is precisely what Johnson performs -- subjectivity reduced to biomarkers. The broccoli, which has no subjectivity, is nonetheless treated as a somatic individual, subjected to ethopolitical protocols. The installation reveals ethopolitics as a system that can operate without a self -- governance of biological matter need not require consciousness.

**Giorgio Agamben, *Homo Sacer: Sovereign Power and Bare Life* [1995]. Trans. Daniel Heller-Roazen. Stanford: Stanford University Press, 1998. ISBN: 978-0-8047-3218-5.**
Agamben radicalises Foucault by arguing that biopower and sovereignty are not sequential historical phases but fundamentally integrated -- "the production of a biopolitical body is the original activity of sovereign power." His central concept is "bare life" (*zoe*): biological life stripped of political qualification (*bios*), exemplified by the Roman figure of *homo sacer* who "may be killed and yet not sacrificed." Bare life exists in an "inclusive exclusion" -- included in the juridical order solely through its capacity to be killed. The "camp" is the paradigmatic biopolitical space, where "inhabitants were stripped of every political status and wholly reduced to bare life." **For Cybroc:** The broccoli is bare life par excellence -- vegetal life (*zoe*) that has never possessed political qualification (*bios*), included in the installation solely through its capacity to be consumed. Its "exercise" is a fiction of *bios* imposed on *zoe*. The three-day duration of the installation functions as a camp in miniature -- a biopolitical space where vegetal bare life is subjected to sovereign protocols it cannot comprehend.

**Roberto Esposito, *Communitas: The Origin and Destiny of Community* [1998]. Trans. Timothy C. Campbell. Stanford: Stanford University Press, 2010. ISBN: 978-0-8047-4647-2. Also: *Immunitas: The Protection and Negation of Life* [2002]. Trans. Zakiya Hanafi. Cambridge: Polity, 2011. ISBN: 978-0-7456-4914-6. Also: *Bios: Biopolitics and Philosophy* [2004]. Trans. Timothy Campbell. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, 2008. ISBN: 978-0-8166-4990-7.**
Esposito's trilogy reconceives biopolitics through the paradigm of immunization. *Communitas* argues that community (*communitas*) is constituted by a shared obligation (*munus* -- gift, duty, obligation), while *Immunitas* argues that modern politics operates through immunization (*immunitas*) -- the exemption from this shared obligation. The tension between community and immunity is the fundamental dynamic of modern biopolitics: "what seems to safeguard the individual and collective body appears to be the same thing that slows down its development, blocking its growth and beyond a certain point, destroying it." Esposito characterises modern biopolitics through the tension between living in community and immunising the population from threats to its health -- an "affirmative biopolitics" that must find balance between these poles. **For Cybroc:** The immunization paradigm directly illuminates the anti-aging project. Johnson's Blueprint is pure *immunitas* -- an attempt to immunise the body against aging, disease, and death, exempting it from the shared biological *munus* of mortality. The broccoli, subjected to immunising protocols (exercise as prophylaxis), reveals what happens when immunisation overreaches: it destroys the very life it seeks to protect. The broccoli's decay despite its "immunisation" through exercise enacts Esposito's warning about autoimmune collapse.

**Achille Mbembe, "Necropolitics." *Public Culture* 15(1): 11--40, 2003. DOI: 10.1215/08992363-15-1-11. Expanded as: *Necropolitics*. Durham: Duke University Press, 2019. ISBN: 978-1-4780-0651-0.**
Mbembe extends Foucault's biopolitics to account for forms of power that Foucault's framework cannot adequately address -- specifically, the politics of death in contexts of colonialism and structural racism. He defines necropolitics as "the use of social and political power to dictate how some people may live and how some must die," creating "deathworlds" -- "new and unique forms of social existence in which vast populations are subjected to living conditions that confer upon them the status of the living dead." The ultimate expression of sovereignty "resides, to a large degree, in the power and the capacity to dictate who may live and who must die." **For Cybroc:** Mbembe's necropolitics provides a crucial counterpoint to the installation's satire. While Bryan Johnson's wealth enables him to pursue radical life extension, Mbembe reminds us that the global distribution of death is structured by race and colonial history. The broccoli's forced exercises in a conference hall at Kyoto, funded by donations, take place in a world where necropolitical forces determine who lives and who dies. The absurdity of investing in a vegetable's "longevity" becomes politically charged when contrasted with the structural conditions under which vast populations are subjected to premature death.

### Bryan Johnson's Blueprint as Biopolitical Self-Governance

The Blueprint protocol represents a unique convergence of multiple biopolitical logics. Johnson is simultaneously: (1) Foucault's disciplined body -- subjected to rigorous anatomo-politics (precise exercise regimes, meal timing, supplement protocols); (2) Rose's somatic individual -- defining selfhood through biomarkers and molecular data; (3) Foucault's *homo oeconomicus* as entrepreneur of the self -- investing financial capital in biological capital; (4) Esposito's immunised subject -- attempting to exempt himself from the shared obligation of mortality. The live-streaming and documentation of the protocol adds a fifth dimension: Johnson subjects himself to what Foucault would recognise as a confessional apparatus, making his biological life perpetually visible and legible. Cybroc transposes all five of these biopolitical logics onto a vegetable, revealing their absurdity through species-displacement.

## 3. MEDICALIZATION, IATROGENESIS, AND THE OPTIMIZATION IMPERATIVE

### Illich and Medical Nemesis

**Ivan Illich, *Limits to Medicine -- Medical Nemesis: The Expropriation of Health*. London: Marion Boyars, 1976. ISBN: 978-0-7145-2993-6. Reprinted Penguin, 1991. ISBN: 978-0-14-013615-9.**
Illich's excoriating critique of modern medicine identifies three forms of iatrogenesis (physician-caused harm). **Clinical iatrogenesis**: the direct injury done to patients by "ineffective, toxic, and unsafe treatments." **Social iatrogenesis**: the medicalization of life itself, where "more and more problems are seen as amenable to medical intervention." **Cultural iatrogenesis**: the destruction of traditional ways of dealing with and making sense of death, pain, and sickness -- medicine's expropriation of the individual's capacity to suffer, heal, and die. Illich opens the book: "The medical establishment has become a major threat to health." His central argument is that industrialised medicine produces dependency, undermining the autonomous capacity of individuals to cope with their own biological reality. **For Cybroc:** The Blueprint protocol is social and cultural iatrogenesis personified. Johnson's regime medicalises every aspect of existence -- sleep, diet, exercise, even the rate of aging itself -- producing the dependency Illich describes. The broccoli, subjected to medicalised protocols it cannot choose or refuse, becomes a figure for cultural iatrogenesis: its "natural" mode of being (growing, photosynthesising, decaying) is expropriated by an external optimisation regime. The rot that inevitably occurs is not a failure of the protocol but the return of the repressed biological reality that Illich warned medicine cannot ultimately suppress.

### The Wellness Syndrome

**Carl Cederstrom and Andre Spicer, *The Wellness Syndrome*. Cambridge: Polity, 2015. ISBN: 978-0-7456-5561-1.**
Cederstrom and Spicer argue that the ever-present pressure to maximise wellness has started to work against us, producing what they call "biomorality" -- "the moral demand to be happy and healthy." They trace how feeling good has become indistinguishable from being good, how visions of social change have been reduced to "dreams of individual transformation," and how political debate has been "replaced by insipid moralising." The goal of combining physical fitness and positive mental attitude to achieve an "ideal performance state" has become a moral imperative for the contemporary subject. The book reveals how the wellness imperative functions as a form of neoliberal self-governance that ultimately reinforces social inequalities by moralising health outcomes. **For Cybroc:** The broccoli's forced exercises literalise the wellness syndrome -- the "biomorality" of optimisation applied to a being for whom it is categorically meaningless. The installation exposes the moral imperative of wellness as structural rather than personal: the apparatus of optimization operates independently of the subject's capacity to benefit.

**Barbara Ehrenreich, *Natural Causes: An Epidemic of Wellness, the Certainty of Dying, and Killing Ourselves to Live Longer*. New York: Twelve, 2018. ISBN: 978-1-4555-3591-0.**
Ehrenreich, writing at age 76, mounts a polemic against wellness culture and the institutions that sustain it. She argues that in many cases, "wellness" is "really about victim-blaming," treating death as a personal failure rather than a natural and inevitable part of life. She traces the surge of interest in physical fitness to the 1980s, when "disillusionment with the counterculture movement led to an inward turn where one could control their own body." Starting with the immune system's paradoxical tendency to promote cancers, Ehrenreich presents "the emerging scientific case for a dystopian view of the body -- not as a well-ordered machine, but as a site of ongoing conflict at the cellular level." She concludes that being old enough to die is "an achievement, not a defeat, and the freedom it brings is worth celebrating." **For Cybroc:** Ehrenreich's "dystopian view of the body" maps precisely onto the broccoli's cellular reality -- an organism in which decay is not a failure of the system but its natural operation. Her critique of wellness as victim-blaming gains absurdist force when applied to a vegetable: the broccoli's rot cannot be blamed on insufficient exercise, yet the installation's apparatus implies precisely this.

### The Pursuit of the Healthy Body as Pathology

The optimization imperative represents what might be called a pathology of health -- a condition in which the pursuit of health itself becomes a source of suffering and constraint. This is not orthorexia (the clinical obsession with "correct" eating) but a broader cultural condition in which the healthy body becomes an impossible standard against which all bodies are measured and found wanting. Johnson's Blueprint, with its 100+ daily supplements and precise biomarker tracking, exemplifies this condition: health is not a state to be achieved but a process of indefinite optimisation, a horizon that recedes as one approaches it. The broccoli, subjected to this apparatus, reveals the optimisation imperative's tautology: it exercises because the protocol demands exercise, not because exercise produces health in any organism that has already been severed from its conditions of life.

## 4. DISABILITY STUDIES AND CRIP THEORY

### Compulsory Able-Bodiedness

**Robert McRuer, *Crip Theory: Cultural Signs of Queerness and Disability*. New York: New York University Press, 2006. ISBN: 978-0-8147-5713-0.**
McRuer's foundational text for crip theory introduces the concept of "compulsory able-bodiedness" -- modelled on Adrienne Rich's "compulsory heterosexuality" -- arguing that like compulsory heterosexuality, compulsory able-bodiedness "functions by covering over, with the appearance of choice, a system in which there actually is no choice." Both disability studies and queer theory are concerned with how bodies and identities are represented as "normal" or as abject. Crip theory is the first framework to analyse thoroughly how these fields inform each other. McRuer builds his argument through case studies showing where compulsory able-bodiedness and heterosexuality are created while disability and queerness are simultaneously contained and constrained. **For Cybroc:** The installation enacts compulsory able-bodiedness in its most extreme form -- an organism that is incapable of "ability" in any meaningful sense is nonetheless forced to perform exercises associated with able-bodied human optimization. The broccoli's "failure" to become healthy through exercise exposes compulsory able-bodiedness as a systemic logic rather than an individual achievement.

### The Curative Imaginary and Crip Time

**Alison Kafer, *Feminist, Queer, Crip*. Bloomington: Indiana University Press, 2013. ISBN: 978-0-253-00934-0.**
Kafer introduces two concepts essential for reading Cybroc. **The "curative imaginary"**: the erroneous assumption that all disabled people long for a "lost whole, pre-illness, pre-disability body." The curative imaginary structures futurity itself -- imagining futures only in terms of the elimination of disability. **"Crip time"**: an alternative temporality that "exists outside of usual modes of experiencing time," resisting the heteronormative temporal logic that "constantly archives for a future that promises employment and marriage and children." Crip time "comes in fits and bursts, with the urgency of an epidemic and the delays of a disabling society." Kafer juxtaposes theories typically discussed in isolation -- environmental justice, reproductive justice, cyborg theory, transgender politics, disability -- to envision new possibilities for crip futures. **For Cybroc:** Johnson's "Don't Die" motto is the curative imaginary writ large -- it imagines a future defined solely by the elimination of aging, the ultimate "disability" in the optimization framework. The broccoli, however, operates in crip time: its three-day decay resists the curative timeline, insisting on a temporality structured by decomposition rather than cure. The installation's real-time decay is crip time made visible -- biological process that cannot be normalised, accelerated, or optimised into the curative imaginary's preferred future.

### Misfits and Embodied Particularity

**Rosemarie Garland-Thomson, "Misfits: A Feminist Materialist Disability Concept." *Hypatia* 26(3): 591--609, 2011. DOI: 10.1111/j.1527-2001.2011.01206.x.**
Garland-Thomson introduces the concept of "misfitting" to describe a dynamic, situated relationship between body and environment. When a particular embodiment encounters an environment not designed for it, a "misfit" occurs. The concept emphasises "the particularity of varying lived embodiments" while maintaining that disability is a social phenomenon -- the result of environmental configurations, not bodily deficiency. Misfitting is "a shifting spatial and perpetual dynamic relationship" -- not a property of the body but an event that occurs in the encounter between body and world. **For Cybroc:** The broccoli is a radical misfit -- a vegetal body placed in an environment (an exercise apparatus, a conference hall, a live-stream platform) designed entirely for human bodies. Every element of the installation produces misfitting: the servo motors impose human movement patterns on a non-human morphology; the exercise protocol assumes a body with muscles, skeleton, and cardiovascular system; the live-stream assumes a performer with agency and intention. The broccoli's radical misfitting does not reveal its deficiency but the environment's -- the optimization apparatus cannot accommodate any body other than the normative human one it was designed for.

### Transhumanised Ableism

**Gregor Wolbring, "The Politics of Ableism." *Development* 51(2): 252--258, 2008. DOI: 10.1057/dev.2008.17.**
Wolbring, a biochemist and disability scholar, argues that ableism should be understood not just in relation to disabled people but as a broader cultural phenomenon. He defines ableism as "a set of beliefs, processes and practices that produce -- based on abilities one exhibits or values -- a particular understanding of oneself, one's body and one's relationship with others of humanity, other species and the environment." Crucially, Wolbring identifies "transhumanised ableism" -- the extension of ableism through enhancement technologies. If enhancement technologies emerge, "remaining merely human could be characterized as 'disabilities,' and refusing biotechnologies would signify actively choosing the disability of being human." **For Cybroc:** Wolbring's "transhumanised ableism" describes exactly the logic the installation satirises. In the Blueprint framework, ordinary aging -- the condition of every living organism -- is treated as a disability to be overcome through technological intervention. The broccoli, subjected to an anti-aging protocol, reveals that transhumanised ableism can be extended to any biological organism: if aging is a disability, then the broccoli is "disabled" by its nature, and the exercise apparatus is its "accommodation."

**Melinda Hall, *The Bioethics of Enhancement: Transhumanism, Disability, and Biopolitics*. Lanham, MD: Lexington Books, 2017. ISBN: 978-1-4985-3348-5.**
Hall draws on Foucault to reveal how disability is central to the bioethics debate over enhancement. She argues that transhumanists "denigrate disabled people by devaluing interdependence and vulnerability" and uncritically adopt the tenets of classical humanism, such that the imagined "cyborg does not celebrate difference, but rather celebrates the maximization and optimization of familiar valued traits such as cognitive ability." The transhumanist framework imposes an "abled-disabled binary" in which "if enhancement technologies emerged, remaining merely human could be characterized as 'disabilities.'" **For Cybroc:** Hall's analysis illuminates how the installation critiques the abled-disabled binary. The broccoli -- which cannot be "abled" or "disabled" in any meaningful sense -- is nonetheless subjected to the enhancement apparatus, revealing the binary's absurdity when applied to organisms outside the human norm.

### The Broccoli as "Crip" Subject Refusing Cure

How longevity optimization extends ableism to all embodiment: The intersection of disability studies and longevity ethics reveals that the anti-aging movement represents the ultimate extension of compulsory able-bodiedness. If aging is pathologised, then every body is always-already disabled -- always falling short of the optimised ideal. Johnson's protocol treats the ordinary biological processes of aging as a disease requiring treatment. The broccoli, in this framework, becomes a "crip" subject -- a body that refuses cure not through agency but through ontological incompatibility with the curative framework. Its decay is not a failure to comply with the exercise protocol but a demonstration that some bodies are constitutively outside the optimization apparatus's reach. The broccoli's "refusal" of cure -- its continued rotting despite exercise -- performs what Kafer and McRuer theorise: that the curative imaginary's promise of wholeness is a fantasy that can never be fulfilled, even in principle.

## 5. PLANT ETHICS AND VEGETAL PHILOSOPHY

### Plant-Thinking and the Vegetal Soul

**Michael Marder, *Plant-Thinking: A Philosophy of Vegetal Life*. New York: Columbia University Press, 2013. ISBN: 978-0-231-16125-1.**
Marder's groundbreaking work puts vegetal life at the forefront of philosophical inquiry. He develops the concept of "plant-thinking" as "the non-cognitive, non-ideational, and non-imagistic mode of thinking proper to plants, as much as the process of bringing human thought itself back to its roots and rendering it plant-like." The book posits a secular, immanent "soul" of plants pertaining to "the elusive life" of flora -- "its precariousness, violability, and its astonishing tenacity and capacity for survival." Part I develops a "Vegetal Anti-Metaphysics" covering the soul and body of the plant; Part II addresses "Vegetal Existentiality" through the time, freedom, and wisdom of plants. Marder also theorises "the logic of totalization" -- the Western metaphysical tendency to reduce vegetal life to a mere resource for human use. **For Cybroc:** Marder's framework poses the installation's deepest ethical challenge. In subjecting the broccoli to human exercise protocols, Cybroc enacts precisely the "logic of totalization" Marder criticises -- treating vegetal life as mere material for human expression. Yet the installation also performs what Marder advocates: attending to the vegetal soul's "precariousness, violability" through the very act of making the broccoli's decay visible and present. The tension between these two readings -- totalization and attention -- is productive rather than resolvable. The broccoli's indifference to its own optimization is itself a form of "plant-thinking" -- a non-cognitive mode of being that resists the cognitive frameworks imposed upon it.

### Plants as Moral Subjects

**Matthew Hall, *Plants as Persons: A Philosophical Botany*. Albany: SUNY Press, 2011. ISBN: 978-1-4384-3428-5.**
Hall surveys Western, Eastern, Pagan, and Indigenous thought for attitudes toward plants, arguing that plants are "other-than-human persons" deserving moral consideration. He finds that the most hierarchical philosophical systems typically place plants at the bottom, but Indigenous and animist traditions provide resources for a more positive view. Hall explains that plants are "active, self-directed, and even intelligent," proposing that "nature is a communion of subjective, collaborative beings." **For Cybroc:** If plants are persons, then subjecting broccoli to forced exercise raises genuine ethical questions that go beyond satire. Hall's framework positions the broccoli not merely as material or medium but as a moral subject whose autonomy (however vegetal) is violated by the apparatus. This reading generates a productive ethical discomfort distinct from the installation's comedic register.

### Plant Intelligence and Sensory Worlds

**Monica Gagliano, M. Renton, N. Depczynski, et al., "Experience teaches plants to learn faster and forget slower in environments where it matters." *Oecologia* 175: 63--72, 2014. DOI: 10.1007/s00442-013-2873-7. Also: Gagliano, M., V.V. Vyazovskiy, A.A. Borbely, M. Grimonprez, and M. Depczynski, "Learning by Association in Plants." *Scientific Reports* 6: 38427, 2016. DOI: 10.1038/srep38427.**
Gagliano's research has generated significant debate by demonstrating elementary forms of learning in plants. The 2014 study showed that Mimosa pudica exhibits habituation -- it learns to stop folding its leaves in response to repeated harmless stimuli, and this learned behavior persists for at least a month. The 2016 study claimed to demonstrate associative learning (Pavlovian conditioning) in pea plants, though subsequent replication attempts have been contested. Regardless of the replication debate, the research has radically expanded scientific consideration of plant cognitive capacities. **For Cybroc:** Gagliano's research is relevant not because the broccoli "learns" its exercises but because it cannot -- it is a severed, non-rooted vegetable. The gap between Gagliano's living, rooted, responsive plants and Cybroc's severed, decaying broccoli measures the distance between plant intelligence research and the installation's absurdist premise. The broccoli is precisely the wrong kind of plant for learning: already harvested, already dying, already incapable of the responses Gagliano documents.

**Natasha Myers, "Becoming Sensor in Sentient Worlds: A More-than-natural History of a Black Oak Savannah." In G. Bakke and M. Peterson (eds.), *Between Matter and Method: Encounters in Anthropology and Art*. London: Bloomsbury, 2017. Also: "From the Anthropocene to the Planthroposcene." In *Ungridding Ecologies*, 2017.**
Myers, an anthropologist, experiments with "protocols for an ungrid-able ecology," proposing what she calls the "Planthroposcene" -- "an aspirational episteme or scene in which we seed solidarities with the plants." Her "becoming sensor" methodology involves "moving with and being moved by the living world, attuning to happenings and becomings" in ecological systems. **For Cybroc:** Myers's "becoming sensor" stands in stark contrast to Cybroc's approach: where Myers advocates attunement and solidarity with plants, Cybroc imposes human protocols on vegetal matter. This contrast is itself productive -- the installation performs the failure of the anthropocentric "gridding" that Myers critiques, revealing what happens when we approach plants not through solidarity but through optimization.

### The Metaphysics of Vegetal Mixture

**Emanuele Coccia, *The Life of Plants: A Metaphysics of Mixture* [*La vie des plantes*, 2016]. Trans. Dylan J. Montanari. Cambridge: Polity, 2019. ISBN: 978-1-5095-3153-0.**
Coccia argues that plants occupy the "fundamental position from which we should analyze all elements of life" because they are the creators of atmosphere itself. His "metaphysics of mixture" proposes that the world is not "a simple collection of objects or a universal space containing all things, but the site of a veritable metaphysical mixture" -- breath constantly connecting all living beings through shared atmosphere. **For Cybroc:** Coccia's metaphysics of mixture has a literal dimension in the installation: the rotting broccoli releases gases, changes the room's atmosphere, and mixes with the conference environment. The broccoli's decay is not isolated within the apparatus but spreads through atmospheric mixture -- a material enactment of Coccia's philosophical claim that vegetal life constitutes the atmosphere we all share.

### Ethics of Bioart and Living Materials

**Nora S. Vaage, "What Ethics for Bioart?" *NanoEthics* 10(1): 87--104, 2016. DOI: 10.1007/s11569-016-0253-6.**
Vaage argues that discussions of ethical issues in bioart typically draw on bioethics frameworks but rarely engage with existing discourses on art and morality from aesthetics. She proposes integrating both frameworks to develop an ethics adequate to bioart's subject matter. Using the Tissue Culture & Art Project as her empirical case, Vaage suggests that "the affective, visceral qualities of living artworks may spur the audience to adjust, revise or develop their personal ethical framework." **For Cybroc:** Vaage's integrated framework is directly applicable. The broccoli's decay provokes visceral response (disgust, discomfort, humour) that is simultaneously aesthetic experience and ethical provocation. The installation introduces a novel ethical dimension that Vaage does not anticipate: working with organisms already "condemned" to consumption (grocery-bought broccoli), raising questions about whether artistic use of already-destined-for-death organisms is ethically distinct from using purpose-bred biological materials.

## 6. CRITICAL ART PRACTICE AND ETHICS OF SATIRE

### Autonomy, Complicity, and Participatory Art

**Claire Bishop, *Artificial Hells: Participatory Art and the Politics of Spectatorship*. London: Verso, 2012. ISBN: 978-1-84467-690-3.**
Bishop provides the first comprehensive historical and theoretical account of participatory art (known in the US as "social practice"). Drawing on Ranciere, she argues that art must maintain "a degree of autonomy and unreadability in order to resist co-option by the political and economic forces intent on imposing a false social consensus." She challenges the tendency to evaluate participatory art on ethical rather than aesthetic criteria, calling for "a less prescriptive approach to art and politics, and for more compelling, troubling, and bolder forms of participatory art and criticism." Bishop's earlier critique in *October* (2004) charged relational art with "collapsing into compensatory entertainment" and ignoring institutional complicity with capitalism. **For Cybroc:** Bishop's framework positions Cybroc as precisely the kind of "troubling" art she advocates -- it maintains autonomy through absurdity (broccoli doing push-ups cannot be easily recuperated into any political programme) while simultaneously making its economic apparatus visible (the donation-to-dinner pipeline). The installation resists both purely ethical and purely aesthetic evaluation, demanding the integrated approach Bishop calls for.

### Distribution of the Sensible and Dissensus

**Jacques Ranciere, *The Politics of Aesthetics: The Distribution of the Sensible* [2000]. Trans. Gabriel Rockhill. London: Continuum, 2004. ISBN: 978-0-8264-7067-6. Also: *Dissensus: On Politics and Aesthetics*. Ed. Steven Corcoran. London: Continuum, 2010. ISBN: 978-1-8470-6445-5.**
Ranciere's central concept is the "distribution of the sensible" (*le partage du sensible*) -- "the way in which roles and modes of participation in a common social world are determined by establishing possible modes of perception." Politics and aesthetics share this function: both redistribute what can be seen, heard, and thought. "Dissensus" is the disruption of a given distribution -- "a dissensual re-configuration of the common experience of the sensible." Art is political not when it carries a political message but when it disrupts the existing partition of the sensible, making visible what was invisible, making audible what was inaudible. Ranciere distinguishes three regimes: the ethical regime, the representative regime, and the aesthetic regime, arguing that the aesthetic regime (which emerges in modernity) is inherently egalitarian because it dismantles hierarchies of subject matter and medium. **For Cybroc:** The installation performs dissensus by making the invisible visible: the absurd logic of optimization, normally concealed within the self-help industry's rhetoric of empowerment, is rendered visible and sensible through the spectacle of a broccoli doing push-ups. The broccoli's exercise disrupts the "distribution of the sensible" that confines optimization to the human realm, revealing its logic as applicable -- and equally absurd -- across species boundaries. Furthermore, the installation enacts what Ranciere calls the aesthetic regime's egalitarianism: a vegetable is given the same "exercise programme" as a tech billionaire, collapsing the hierarchy that would separate human enhancement from vegetal manipulation.

### Dialogical Aesthetics

**Grant Kester, *Conversation Pieces: Community and Communication in Modern Art*. Berkeley: University of California Press, 2004. ISBN: 978-0-520-23839-8.**
Kester develops the concept of "dialogical aesthetics" -- art practices that create meaning through creative dialogue across social boundaries. Drawing on Bakhtin and Habermas, he examines works that function as "conversation pieces," generating understanding through sustained engagement rather than shock or spectacle. Kester's framework privileges process over product and emphasises the ethical dimensions of artistic encounter. **For Cybroc:** The live-stream's chat function creates a dialogical space around the installation, but one that is fundamentally asymmetrical -- the broccoli cannot participate in dialogue. The "conversation" is entirely among humans, about a non-human subject that is oblivious to the discussion. This asymmetry transforms Kester's dialogical aesthetics into what might be called "monological aesthetics" -- a conversation directed at an entity incapable of response, exposing the limits of dialogical models when applied to non-human or more-than-human art practices.

### When Does Satire of Optimization Become Complicity with Optimization?

This is the ethical crux of the installation. Cybroc satirises the optimization imperative by subjecting broccoli to Bryan Johnson's exercise protocols, but the very act of building the apparatus, live-streaming the performance, collecting donations, and generating attention reproduces the logics of optimisation culture: metric-driven productivity, constant surveillance, platform-mediated attention capture, and the conversion of "content" (even absurd content) into economic value. Bishop's framework suggests this complicity is not a failure but a feature: art that maintains too-clean a separation from the systems it critiques risks the self-satisfied purity that Bishop criticises in ethically-motivated participatory art. Ranciere would argue that the installation's political efficacy lies precisely in its "undecidability" -- its refusal to resolve the tension between critique and complicity, producing genuine dissensus rather than a clear political message. The installation's self-consuming economic circuit (donations fund a dinner consumed by the artists who built the apparatus) makes this complicity visible and structural rather than hidden, transforming it from a flaw into an element of the work's meaning.

## 7. HEIDEGGER AND MORTALITY

### Being-toward-death (Sein-zum-Tode)

**Martin Heidegger, *Being and Time* [*Sein und Zeit*, 1927]. Trans. John Macquarrie and Edward Robinson. Oxford: Blackwell, 1962. Esp. Division II, Chapters 1--3 (sections 45--65).**
Heidegger's analysis of death is central to his account of authentic existence. Death is Dasein's "ownmost possibility" -- it is **non-relational** (nobody can die in one's place), **certain** (inevitable), **indefinite** (unknowable in its timing), and **not to be outstripped** (the possibility that makes all other possibilities possible). "Being-toward-death" (*Sein-zum-Tode*) is the structure by which Dasein relates to this possibility. Heidegger distinguishes between authentic and inauthentic modes of being-toward-death:

**Inauthentic being-toward-death** is characterised by "falling" (*Verfallen*) -- Dasein flees from the confrontation with its own mortality into the anonymous "they" (*das Man*), treating death as something that happens to "one" but not to me, personally: "one dies" rather than "I will die." The "they" domesticates death, transforms it into a public event that is always someone else's.

**Authentic being-toward-death** involves "anticipatory resoluteness" (*vorlaufende Entschlossenheit*) -- running ahead into the possibility of death without fleeing from it. The anticipation of death "individualizes Dasein down to itself," liberating it from the anonymous "they" and enabling authentic existence. In anticipatory resoluteness, one integrates projects and dispositions to achieve "the constancy of the self" -- a stable, individualised way of being-in-the-world. Death is not something to be feared or avoided but to be held open as a possibility that gives life its urgency, meaning, and individuation.

Heidegger insists that being-toward-death is not a morbid fascination but the condition of possibility for authentic selfhood: only by confronting mortality can Dasein grasp its existence as finite, singular, and its own. Time itself "finds its meaning in death," and temporality is "understood only from a finite or mortal vantage."

### "Don't Die" as Negation of Authentic Being-toward-Death

Johnson's motto "Don't Die" is, in Heideggerian terms, the purest expression of inauthentic being-toward-death. It is the ultimate flight from mortality -- not merely the everyday "one doesn't die" but an active, technologically mediated refusal to confront death as one's ownmost possibility. "Don't Die" attempts to outstrip the possibility that Heidegger says cannot be outstripped. Where Heidegger argues that anticipating death individualises Dasein, Johnson's programme attempts to de-individualise death -- to make it a technical problem solvable through sufficient data, supplements, and exercise.

The Blueprint protocol enacts what Heidegger would recognise as "falling" at its most extreme: not the everyday falling of *das Man* who simply does not think about death, but an active, systematic, technologically sophisticated avoidance of the very possibility that structures authentic existence. Johnson does not merely cover over death; he builds an elaborate apparatus for its negation.

### The Broccoli's Decay as Vorlaufen zum Tode

The broccoli's three-day decomposition performs what Johnson's protocol cannot: authentic being-toward-death. Not because the broccoli "chooses" to confront death (it has no Dasein, no capacity for choice), but because its biological process enacts the temporal structure that Heidegger describes. The decay is:

- **Ownmost**: no other broccoli can rot in its place.
- **Non-relational**: the decay occurs regardless of the apparatus, the audience, or the protocols.
- **Certain**: irreversible once begun.
- **Indefinite in its timing**: the exact moment of final decomposition cannot be predicted, only that it will arrive.
- **Not to be outstripped**: the exercises cannot prevent or even delay the rotting process.

The broccoli's decay is *Vorlaufen* -- a running-ahead toward death that is not a choice but an ontological condition. The exercises imposed upon it are the apparatus of flight (*Verfallen*), the installation's literal staging of inauthenticity. The humour of the piece arises from the gap between the apparatus of flight (the exercises, the live-stream, the optimization protocols) and the inexorable running-ahead of the decay.

There is a deeper irony: the broccoli achieves what Heidegger describes as the condition for authentic existence -- confrontation with mortality -- precisely because it lacks Dasein. It cannot flee from death because it cannot conceive of death; it cannot be inauthentic because it has no capacity for authenticity. The broccoli is always-already in the condition that Heidegger argues humans must achieve through resolute anticipation. The installation thus poses a disturbing question: is the most "authentic" relation to death one that requires no consciousness at all? Is the vegetal mode of being-toward-death -- passive, inevitable, without anxiety -- the limit case that reveals the impossibility of Heidegger's project?

### Temporality and the Conference Frame

The three-day frame of ALIFE 2025 in Kyoto functions as a Heideggerian temporal horizon. Conference-goers know from the outset that the broccoli will be dead (fully decomposed) by the conference's end. This foreknowledge structures their experience of the installation in precisely the way Heidegger describes: anticipation of the end gives each moment its significance. The daily changes in the broccoli's condition -- increasingly visible rot, changing colour, emerging smell -- mark the passage of time in a way that the conference schedule does not. The broccoli's time is biological time, not institutional time, and the two temporalities run in parallel: the conference proceeds according to its schedule while the broccoli proceeds according to its decay.

## CROSS-CUTTING THEMES AND CONNECTIONS TO CYBROC

### Theme 1: The Optimised Body as Biopolitical Subject
The convergence of Foucault (biopower), Rose (ethopolitics/somatic individuality), and the disability studies critique (McRuer, Kafer, Wolbring) reveals that the anti-aging movement represents a new form of biopolitical governance in which the individual voluntarily subjects themselves to optimisation protocols, internalising the logic of population management as personal health practice. The broccoli's forced exercises make this governance visible by applying it to a subject incapable of self-governance.

### Theme 2: Immunisation and Its Failures
Esposito's immunisation paradigm and Ehrenreich's critique of wellness culture converge on a central insight: the attempt to immunise the body against mortality produces pathological effects -- autoimmune responses in which the protective mechanism destroys what it seeks to protect. The broccoli's decay despite exercise is the installation's enactment of autoimmune collapse.

### Theme 3: Crip Time vs. Optimisation Time
The temporal structure of the installation stages a conflict between two modes of time: the optimisation time of the exercise protocol (rhythmic, measurable, oriented toward improvement) and the crip time of biological decay (irregular, unpredictable, oriented toward dissolution). The broccoli exists in both temporalities simultaneously, performing exercises in optimisation time while rotting in crip time.

### Theme 4: Plant Ethics and the Limits of Satire
Marder, Hall, and Gagliano's work on plant ethics introduces an ethical dimension that exceeds the installation's satirical framework. If plants possess some form of vegetal subjectivity, then subjecting broccoli to forced exercise is not merely funny but ethically ambiguous -- a tension the installation productively refuses to resolve.

### Theme 5: Complicity and Dissensus
Bishop and Ranciere provide frameworks for understanding the installation's relationship to the systems it critiques. The donation economy, the live-stream, and the attention capture are not failures of the critique but its medium -- the installation's meaning arises from its irreducible complicity with the logics of optimisation, spectacle, and capital.

### Theme 6: The Impossibility of "Don't Die"
Heidegger's being-toward-death, Illich's iatrogenesis, and the crip theory critique of the curative imaginary converge on the impossibility of Johnson's imperative. "Don't Die" is simultaneously an inauthentic flight from mortality (Heidegger), a cultural iatrogenesis that expropriates the capacity to die meaningfully (Illich), and the curative imaginary's most extreme expression (Kafer). The broccoli's rot enacts all three critiques simultaneously.
