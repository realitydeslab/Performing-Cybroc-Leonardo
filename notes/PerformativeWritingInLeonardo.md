# Performative Writing in Leonardo Journal: Deep Research Notes

## 1. Leonardo Journal: Article Types and Editorial Policy

### Article Types (Current, 2025)
Leonardo accepts several article types, each with specific word limits (articles exceeding by >5% are rejected before peer review):

- **General Article** (~3,500 words): Standard scholarly article
- **Statement** (~2,000 words, ≤2 pages, up to 3 illustrations): Short-form position piece. Print publication in <18 months
- **Work Bench** (formerly "Artists' Articles"): Articles written by creative practitioners offering intimate insights into individual cross-disciplinary processes. Recognizes that creativity is not confined to the arts
- **Leosphere**: Section exploring themes and inquiries reflecting the Leonardo community. "Reflections on the Leosphere" and "Leosphere in Action" subsections
- **Special Sections**: Guest-edited thematic collections (open call available)
- **Leonardo Gallery**: Curated first-person artist statements with images

### Writing Style Guidelines (from leonardo.info/preparing-your-materials-journals)
- "Use academic, American English"
- Minimize jargon, passive voice, and unexplained acronyms
- Italicize artwork and installation titles; software in roman font
- Serial commas; international date formats (e.g., "1 January 2024")
- Keep formatting minimal
- Abstract: max 120 words, third-person, present tense
- Keywords: 5–7 terms
- References: Chicago Manual of Style, 18th ed., note style. Bracketed numbers [1], [2]
- Unnumbered section headings with indicated levels (<1>, <2>, <3>)

### Key Observation on Voice
Leonardo's guidelines say "academic" English but do NOT prohibit first-person voice. The Work Bench section explicitly features "articles written by creative practitioners" — this is the category that accommodates first-person performative writing. The journal's founding mission (by Frank Malina, 1968) was to give artists a platform to describe their own work, which inherently involves first-person voice.

### Submission Process
- Both solicited and unsolicited submissions accepted
- Authors encouraged to submit preliminary outlines for editorial feedback first
- 2–3 peer reviewers per manuscript (3–4 months review)
- Accepted articles appear on "Just Accepted" page within 4–6 months
- Print publication typically >3 years (except Statements: <18 months)
- Max 2 articles under consideration at any time

## 2. Specific Leonardo Papers Using Performative/First-Person Writing

### 2.1 Eduardo Kac, "GFP Bunny"
- **Citation**: Kac, E. "GFP Bunny." *Leonardo* 36, no. 2 (2003): 97–102.
- **DOI**: 10.1162/002409403321554125
- **Writing style**: First-person artist narrative. Kac writes: "I will never forget the moment when I first held her in my arms, in Jouy-en-Josas, France, on April 29, 2000." The personal voice blends with theoretical discussion of transgenic art.
- **Relevance**: Paradigmatic example of artist-as-author in Leonardo. The paper documents the creation of Alba (fluorescent rabbit) while simultaneously theorizing bio-art ethics. Personal narrative anchors philosophical argument.

### 2.2 Oron Catts & Ionat Zurr, "Growing Semi-Living Sculptures: The Tissue Culture & Art Project"
- **Citation**: Catts, O. and Zurr, I. "Growing Semi-Living Sculptures: The Tissue Culture & Art Project." *Leonardo* 35, no. 4 (2002): 365–370.
- **DOI**: 10.1162/002409402760181177 (estimated)
- **Writing style**: Artist-researchers documenting their tissue culture art practice. First-person plural ("we") describing laboratory-studio processes. Hybrid voice combining biological methodology with artistic reflection.
- **Relevance**: Model for practice-based bio-art writing where the documentation of process IS the intellectual contribution. Zurr later guest-edited "Agency in Movement" special section (Leonardo Vol. 47, 2014).

### 2.3 Ziggy O'Reilly, Christina Chau, Nathan Thompson, Guy Ben-Ary, "Bioengineered Living Entities in Art: Aliveness, Duration, and Movement in Bricolage"
- **Citation**: O'Reilly, Z., Chau, C., Thompson, N., and Ben-Ary, G. "Bioengineered Living Entities in Art: Aliveness, Duration, and Movement in Bricolage." *Leonardo* 56, no. 5 (2023): 496–500.
- **DOI**: 10.1162/leon_a_02402
- **Writing style**: Artist-researchers documenting kinetic biological artwork using stem cell technologies. Heart muscle cells from human donors create living entities suspended in an incubator, visible to gallery visitors who watch cells generating and moving independently.
- **Relevance**: Recent (2023) example of bio-art artist article in Leonardo. Documents a work where biological material performs — directly comparable to Cybroc's vegetable performers.

### 2.4 Elia Vargas & Danielle Siembieda, "Crude Illumination: A Crude Oil Art Inspection"
- **Citation**: Vargas, E. and Siembieda, D. "Crude Illumination: A Crude Oil Art Inspection." *Leonardo* 54, no. 2 (2021): 208–209.
- **DOI**: 10.1162/leon_a_01978
- **Writing style**: Hybrid research-art practice. Uses "art inspection" as creative framework — Siembieda acts as "Art Inspector" evaluating Vargas's crude oil practice. Performative framing where the critical assessment IS the artwork.
- **Relevance**: Example of two-voice structure (artist + inspector/critic) within a Leonardo article. The paper performs the work it describes.

### 2.5 Claudia Núñez-Pacheco & Jorge Olivares-Retamal, "Art as Refuge: The Symbolic Transformation of an Electronic Installation in the Midst of Chile's Social Unrest"
- **Citation**: Núñez-Pacheco, C. and Olivares-Retamal, J. "Art as Refuge: The Symbolic Transformation of an Electronic Installation in the Midst of Chile's Social Unrest." *Leonardo* 54, no. 3 (2021): 313–314.
- **DOI**: 10.1162/leon_a_02033
- **Writing style**: Position statement documenting transformation of interactive installation from pedagogical tool to political performance during 2019 Chilean unrest. First-person practitioner account of how context transformed meaning.
- **Relevance**: Shows how Leonardo accepts papers where real-world events transform artwork meaning — the documentation performs the political transformation it describes.

### 2.6 Juniper Harrower et al., "The Algae Society BioArt Design Lab: Exploring Multispecies Entanglements and Making Kin with Algae"
- **Citation**: Harrower, J. et al. "The Algae Society BioArt Design Lab: Exploring Multispecies Entanglements and Making Kin with Algae." *Leonardo* 55, no. 4 (2022): 332–336.
- **DOI**: 10.1162/leon_a_02191 (estimated)
- **Writing style**: Collective first-person. "A collective collage" of evolving artistic works where founding members reflect on each researcher's process, materiality, and aesthetic considerations. Manifesto-like format.
- **Relevance**: Recent example of collective/polyphonic voice in Leonardo. Multiple practitioners co-authoring a reflexive account of multispecies art-science practice.

### 2.7 "Entangled Poetics: Two Bioartists in the Anthropocene"
- **Citation**: *Leonardo* 56, no. 4 (2023): 379–.
- **DOI**: (available at direct.mit.edu/leon)

## 3. Theory of Performative Writing

### 3.1 Della Pollock, "Performing Writing" (1998)
- **Citation**: Pollock, D. "Performing Writing." In *The Ends of Performance*, edited by Peggy Phelan and Jill Lane, 73–103. New York: NYU Press, 1998.
- **ISBN**: 978-0-8147-6646-0
- **Six characteristics of performative writing**:
  1. **Evocative**: Evoking worlds of memory, pleasure, sensation, imagination, affect, insight
  2. **Metonymic**: Self-consciously partial, inviting laughter and transformation
  3. **Subjective**: Centering the personal "I" against objective third-person norms
  4. **Nervous**: Unsettled, breaking linear courses, flowing moment to moment
  5. **Citational**: Figuring writing as rewriting; repetition as performative
  6. **Consequential**: Projecting new modes of being and relating
- "Writing as doing displaces writing as meaning. [...] Performativity describes a fundamentally material practice."
- **Key insight**: Performative writing "is not a genre or fixed form but a way of describing what some good writing does."

### 3.2 Della Pollock, "The Performative 'I'" (2007)
- **Citation**: Pollock, D. "The Performative 'I'." *Cultural Studies ↔ Critical Methodologies* 7, no. 3 (2007): 239–255.
- **DOI**: 10.1177/1532708606288649
- **Key argument**: The "performative 'I'" is the active voice of first person in academic writing, contrasted with the "passionate 'I'" that students are taught to suppress. Performative writing deploys the first person as a way of doing research, not just reporting it.

### 3.3 Peggy Phelan, *Unmarked: The Politics of Performance* (1993)
- **Citation**: Phelan, P. *Unmarked: The Politics of Performance*. London: Routledge, 1993.
- **ISBN**: 978-0-415-06822-2
- **Key arguments**:
  - "Performance's only life is in the present"
  - Performance "cannot be saved, recorded, documented, or otherwise participate in the circulation of representations"
  - Writing about performance is itself a performative act that transforms the event
  - Different kinds of writing can "retain the ephemerality of performance"
- **Relevance**: Foundational text for understanding why documentation of performance art necessarily involves performative writing — the act of writing IS an extension of the performance.

### 3.4 Brad Haseman, "A Manifesto for Performative Research" (2006)
- **Citation**: Haseman, B. "A Manifesto for Performative Research." *Media International Australia* 118, no. 1 (2006): 98–106.
- **DOI**: 10.1177/1329878X0611800113
- **Key arguments**:
  - Practice-led research constitutes a third research paradigm alongside qualitative and quantitative
  - Named "Performative Research" after J.L. Austin's speech act theory
  - "Intrinsically experimental" — involves creation of artistic forms as research
  - Not about finding solutions but "diving into practice and seeing what emerges"
  - Applications beyond arts into creative and cultural industries
- **Relevance**: Theoretical justification for the entire approach of the Cybroc paper — the paper performs the research it documents.

### 3.5 Ronald Pelias, "Performative Writing as Scholarship" (2005)
- **Citation**: Pelias, R. J. "Performative Writing as Scholarship: An Apology, an Argument, an Anecdote." *Cultural Studies ↔ Critical Methodologies* 5, no. 4 (2005): 415–424.
- **DOI**: 10.1177/1532708605279694
- **Key argument**: Defends performative writing as legitimate academic scholarship. Makes the case through three modes — apology, argument, anecdote — thereby performing its own thesis.

### 3.6 Barbara Bolt, "Artistic Research: A Performative Paradigm?" (2016)
- **Citation**: Bolt, B. "Artistic Research: A Performative Paradigm?" *PARSE Journal* 3 (2016).
- **URL**: https://parsejournal.com/article/artistic-research-a-performative-paradigm/
- **Key arguments**: "The performative paradigm operates according to repetition with difference, which represents the generative potential of artistic research." Links Haseman's framework to broader artistic research discourse.

## 4. Performative Writing in Other Art Journals

### 4.1 Performance Research
- Founded 1996. Published by Routledge/Taylor & Francis.
- **Explicitly supports performative writing**: "combines writings and works for the page in an interplay of analysis, anecdote, polemic and criticism; interweaving the oblique with the conflicting, the pivotal with the resistant, and the eclectic with the indispensable."
- "Resists disconnected, disembodied, and disinterested forms of scholarship, preferring instead the possibilities of imagining the journal as a dynamic space of performance."
- Peer-reviewed with guest editors per thematic issue.
- **Most receptive** of the art journals to experimental writing formats.

### 4.2 TDR: The Drama Review
- Published by MIT Press (like Leonardo). Founded 1955.
- Historically features performance documentation, artist interviews, and critical essays.
- First-person accounts of performance practice common.

### 4.3 October
- Published by MIT Press. Founded 1976.
- Theoretically rigorous art criticism. Less experimental in format but influential in art-critical voice.
- Key figures: Rosalind Krauss, Hal Foster, Benjamin Buchloh.

### 4.4 Art Journal (College Art Association)
- Features artist pages, critical essays, and portfolio sections.
- Roy Ascott published "Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?" in Art Journal 49:3 (1990). DOI: 10.1080/00043249.1990.10792697

### 4.5 JAWS: Journal of Arts Writing by Students
- Published by Intellect. Publishes "writing about art and writing as art."
- Welcomes practice-based research, visual essays, reviews.

### 4.6 Journal of Writing in Creative Practice
- Official organ of Writing PAD network (Intellect).
- Explores "thinking through writing as a parallel to visual discourse in art and design practice."

## 5. Autoethnography and Practice-Based Research

### Key Frameworks
- **Autoethnography in performance studies**: Acknowledges researcher and audience having equal weight. The performed "self" through writing creates embodied experience for writer and reader.
- **Practice-based research**: "Research process entirely framed as artistic practice." Goes by many names: arts-based research, practice-led research, studio-based research.
- **Performative research paradigm**: Creates new artistic forms to carry out intrinsically experiential practice-led research, understood in terms of the "performative force of art."

### Key Texts
- Borgdorff, H. *The Conflict of the Faculties: Perspectives on Artistic Research and Academia*. Leiden: Leiden University Press, 2012.
- Hannula, M., Suoranta, J., and Vadén, T. *Artistic Research Methodology: Narrative, Power and the Public*. New York: Peter Lang, 2014.
- Schwab, M. and Borgdorff, H., eds. *The Exposition of Artistic Research: Publishing Art in the Academy*. Leiden: Leiden University Press, 2014.

## 6. Implications for the Cybroc Paper

### The paper's performative writing strategy is well-supported by precedent:

1. **Work Bench category**: The Cybroc paper fits naturally in Leonardo's "Work Bench" section (formerly "Artists' Articles"), which explicitly features creative practitioners documenting their cross-disciplinary processes.

2. **Two-voice structure has precedent**: Vargas & Siembieda's "Crude Illumination" uses artist + inspector voices. The Cybroc paper's first-person narrative + critique inserts follows this model.

3. **Bio-art documentation tradition**: Kac, Catts/Zurr, O'Reilly et al. all use first-person to document bio-art in Leonardo. The Cybroc paper continues this lineage.

4. **Theoretical justification**: Pollock's performative writing framework (evocative, metonymic, subjective, nervous, citational, consequential) provides the theoretical basis. Haseman's "Performative Research" paradigm legitimizes the approach as a third research methodology.

5. **The decay narrative mirrors Pollock's "nervous" writing**: Pollock's characteristic of "nervous" writing — unsettled, flowing moment to moment — maps perfectly onto the Cybroc paper's 3-day decay arc structure.

6. **Performance Research as backup venue**: If Leonardo's word count proves restrictive (~3,500 words), Performance Research explicitly welcomes experimental performative writing and may offer more latitude.
