## PART 1: LEONARDO'S EDITORIAL FRAMEWORK AND WRITING POLICIES

### 1.1 Founding Mission: Artists Writing About Their Own Work

Leonardo was founded in 1968 in Paris by kinetic artist and astronautical pioneer Frank Malina, who "saw the need for a journal to serve as an international channel of communication among artists, with emphasis on the writings of artists who use science and developing technologies in their work." The concept was modeled on scientific journals: "a publication by serious artists with subject integrity secured by the same kind of peer review of articles that is common in scientific journals." The foundational aim includes "the documentation of personal and innovative technologies developed by artists, similar to the way in which findings of scientists are documented in journal publications."

**This mission statement is crucial: from its inception, Leonardo was designed to publish artist-authored accounts of their own practice -- a structural commitment to first-person practitioner voice.**

### 1.2 Current Article Types and Word Limits

| Category | Word Count | Figures |
|---|---|---|
| Research Article | 6,000-8,000 | 8-12 |
| Artist's Statement | 2,000-3,000 | 6-10 |
| Review/Essay | 4,000-6,000 | 4-8 |
| Commentary | 1,500-2,500 | 2-4 |
| Interview | 3,000-5,000 | 4-6 |

The "Artist's Statement" category (2,000-3,000 words) is specifically designed for creative practitioners.

### 1.3 Writing Style Requirements

- "Academic American English required"
- "Minimize jargon, passive voice, and unexplained acronyms"
- Abstracts: "max 120 words, third person, present tense"
- **The guidelines do NOT prohibit first-person writing in the body of the article.** Third-person requirement applies only to the abstract.

### 1.4 The "Work Bench" Section (formerly "Artists' Articles")

As of Volume 58 (2025), Leonardo renamed "Artists' Articles" to "Work Bench":

> "The transformation of the section previously known as 'Artists' Articles' into 'Work Bench' symbolizes a broader recognition that creativity is not confined to the arts; it is a fundamental aspect of all disciplines."

The section "features articles written by creative practitioners, offering intimate insights into individual cross-disciplinary processes." The word **"intimate"** signals that personal, practitioner-centered voice is editorially valued.

### 1.5 Other Journal Sections

- **Showcase** (replacing "Gallery"): curated artist projects
- **Leosphere**: cross-disciplinary themes
- **FOCUS**: themed section inviting multiple perspectives

### 1.6 Roger Malina and Editorial Philosophy

Roger Malina (son of founder Frank Malina) served as editor-in-chief since 1982. His father's founding vision -- artists should write about their own work the way scientists do -- has been the continuous editorial philosophy.

## PART 2: SPECIFIC EXAMPLES OF PERFORMATIVE/EXPERIMENTAL WRITING IN LEONARDO

### 2.1 First-Person Artist Narrative

**Eduardo Kac, "GFP Bunny"**
- Leonardo 36:2 (2003): 97-102. DOI: 10.1162/002409403321152275
- "I will never forget the moment I first held the bunny in my arms, in Jouy-en-Josas, France, on 29 April 2000."
- Deeply personal, emotionally engaged first-person writing as peer-reviewed article.

**Oron Catts & Ionat Zurr, "Growing Semi-Living Sculptures"**
- Leonardo 35:4 (2002): 365-370. DOI: 10.1162/002409402760181123
- Artists' Articles section. Practitioner documentation of tissue culture art.
- Boundary between artwork documentation and critical essay deliberately blurred.

**George Gessert, "Notes on Genetic Art"**
- Leonardo 26:3 (1993): 205-211. DOI: 10.2307/1575812
- First-person artist essay on plant breeding as art. "Domesticated ornamental plants, pets, and other organisms constitute unacknowledged genetic folk art."
- **Most relevant plant-focused precedent for Cybroc.**

**Marta de Menezes, "The Artificial Natural: Manipulating Butterfly Wing Patterns for Artistic Purposes"**
- Leonardo 36:1 (2003): 29-32
- Artist-authored account of modifying live butterfly wing patterns through microsurgery.

**Stelarc, "Prosthetics, Robotics and Remote Existence: Postevolutionary Strategies"**
- Leonardo 24:5 (1991): 591-595
- Performance artist writing about body-technology works including Third Hand and body suspensions.

**Vera Molnar, "Toward Aesthetic Guidelines for Paintings with the Aid of a Computer"**
- Leonardo 8:3 (1975)
- First-person artist essay about computational art practice.

**Vera Molnar, "'My Mother's Letters': Simulation by Computer"**
- Leonardo 28:3 (1995)
- Personal/autobiographical computational art. Recreating her mother's handwriting.

### 2.2 Hybrid Voice: Artist + Critic / Personal + Theoretical

**Victoria Vesna, "Toward a Third Culture: Being In Between"**
- Leonardo 34:2 (2001): 121-125. DOI: 10.1162/002409401750184726
- Hybrid voice blending personal reflection as artist-academic with theoretical argument about C.P. Snow's "two cultures."

**Christa Sommerer & Laurent Mignonneau, "Modeling the Emergence of Complexity"**
- Leonardo 35:2 (2002): 161-169
- Artist-researchers bridging personal creative practice and theoretical analysis.

**"Entangled Poetics: Two Bioartists in the Anthropocene"**
- Leonardo 56:4 (2023): 379-382. DOI: 10.1162/leon_a_02398
- Examines intersection of poetics and bio-art practice. Writing about art engaged with literary form.

### 2.3 Paper Structure That Mirrors/Performs the Artwork

**Showcase: Leonardo@Djerassi 2024**
- Leonardo 58:3 (2025): 258-266. DOI: 10.1162/leon_a_02688
- First-person artist statements: "In the summer of 2005, with only a digital camera and hand-held GPS unit, I arrived in India to map the world's most densely populated river basin."
- Recent example: highly personal, narrative-driven first-person writing in Leonardo 2025.

**Elia Vargas's hybrid research art practice (Leonardo 54:2)**
- Uses "performative in-spection" methodology. The analytical method itself described as performative.

### 2.4 Roy Ascott

Honorary editor of Leonardo. Guest Editor, "Connectivity: Art and Interactive Telecommunications," Leonardo 24:2 (1991). Theoretical writing operating at intersection of manifesto, theory, and practice documentation.

### 2.5 ALife and Generative Art

**Nell Tenhaaf, "Art Embodies A-Life: The VIDA Competition"**
- Leonardo 41:1 (2008): 6-15. DOI: 10.1162/leon.2008.41.1.6

**Celestino Soddu, "New Naturality: A Generative Approach to Art and Design"**
- Leonardo 35:3 (2002): 291-294. DOI: 10.1162/002409402760105299

**Harold Cohen and AARON**
- Leonardo 54:4 (2021): 412-. Documentation of 40+ year creative partnership with AI program.

**Ken Rinaldo, "Technology Recapitulates Phylogeny"**
- Leonardo 31:5 (1998). Artist-editor writing on artificial life art.

**Rafael Lozano-Hemmer, "Perverting Technological Correctness"**
- Leonardo (1995). Artist writing on interactive installation.

### 2.6 Leonardo Music Journal (LMJ)

LMJ (1991-2020, merged into Leonardo) published "a collection of unique writing by important but generally under-published artists" with "enormous value to those studying experimental music and sound art." Included audio CDs alongside articles.

**David Dunn, "Music, Language, and Environment"** - LMJ 9 (1999/2000)

### 2.7 Leonardo Electronic Almanac (LEA)

Most explicitly experimental Leonardo publication. Invites papers on "New Media Poetry and Poetics," "codework," "hybrid digital and spatial publishing platforms."

## PART 3: PERFORMATIVE WRITING IN ART JOURNALS BROADLY

### 3.1 Performance Research

Founded 1996. "Resists disconnected, disembodied, and disinterested forms of scholarship." Published landmark special issue on "Performance Writing" (Vol. 1, No. 3, 1996). Ric Allsopp: "Performance writing is an unstable and exploratory term that attempts to hold in tension both writing and its performance."

### 3.2 October

MIT Press. Founded by Krauss and Michelson. Emphasizes "primacy of text and writer's freedom of discourse." Rigorous theory that performs its own arguments.

### 3.3 TDR: The Drama Review

Editorially curated rather than conventionally peer-reviewed, allowing greater latitude for experimental formats.

### 3.4 Art Journal (CAA)

Welcomes "essays, features, interviews, forums, and other projects." "Projects" signals openness to non-standard formats.

### 3.5 Journal for Artistic Research (JAR)

Most formally experimental. "Abandons traditional journal article format." Publishes "expositions" rather than articles on dynamic online canvas with text, image, audio, video.

### 3.6 PAJ: A Journal of Performance and Art

MIT Press. Intersection of performance and visual art. Artist writings, scripts, hybrid formats.

## PART 4: THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS

### 4.1 Della Pollock, "Performing Writing" (1998)

In *The Ends of Performance* (NYU Press). Performative writing "is not a genre or fixed form but a way of describing what some good writing does." Six characteristics: **evocative, metonymic, subjective, nervous, citational, consequential.**

### 4.2 Peggy Phelan, *Unmarked* (1993)

"Performance's only life is in the present." To document performance is to transform it. "Different kinds of writing can retain the ephemerality of performance."

### 4.3 Ric Allsopp, "Performance Writing" (1996)

"An unstable and exploratory term" that "problematizes and widens the discourse surrounding the textuality of contemporary arts practice."

### 4.4 Ronald Pelias, "Performative Writing as Scholarship" (2005)

*Cultural Studies / Critical Methodologies* 5(4). Argues for performative writing as legitimate scholarship through "an apology, an argument, an anecdote."

## PART 5: KEY ARGUMENTS FOR THE CYBROC PAPER'S FORMAT

1. **Leonardo's founding mission explicitly centers artist voice.** First-person artist narrative is the journal's raison d'être.

2. **"Work Bench" explicitly values "intimate insights."** Personal, practitioner-centered voice is editorially valued.

3. **"Artist's Statement" exists as a formal article type.** Institutional acceptance of practitioner voice.

4. **Substantial precedent:** Kac, Catts/Zurr, Gessert, de Menezes, Stelarc, Molnar, Vesna, Sommerer/Mignonneau -- all published first-person practitioner accounts.

5. **Third person required only for abstract, not body text.** The dual-voice structure is consistent with guidelines.

6. **Leonardo publishes work foregrounding its own methodology.** "Performative in-spection" (Vargas) and creative methodologies in recent issues.

7. **Broader art journal ecology supports performative writing.** Performance Research, JAR, TDR, Art Journal all publish where writing style = intellectual contribution.

8. **The "Note on the Structure of This Text" makes performative strategy explicit and scholarly** -- exactly what Pollock's theory calls for.
