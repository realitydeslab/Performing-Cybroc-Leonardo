## 1. HISTORY OF BIO-ART AS PRACTICE

### Key Practitioners and Works

**Eduardo Kac** coined "transgenic art." *Genesis* (1999) was the first artwork to use synthetic DNA. *GFP Bunny* (2000) -- Alba, an albino rabbit engineered with GFP -- became the most widely discussed bio-art work. The custody dispute when INRA refused to release the rabbit became part of the artwork itself.

**Oron Catts and Ionat Zurr / SymbioticA / TC&A** established their collaboration in 1996 and founded SymbioticA at UWA in 2000 -- first art research laboratory in a biology department. Key works: *Semi-Living Worry Dolls* (2000), *Pig Wings Project* (2000--01, MoMA collection), *Victimless Leather* (2004). At MoMA's "Design and the Elastic Mind" (2008), curator Paola Antonelli had to "kill" the growing jacket when cells grew uncontrollably. Catts noted this fit their "overarching goal to present the end of their projects in ways that remind people that these works are/were alive."

**Joe Davis** -- considered first bio-artist. *Microvenus* (1986) with Dana Boyd -- first non-biological DNA message inserted into E. coli.

**Stelarc** -- body/machine interface since 1970s. 25 body suspension performances, *Third Hand*, *Amplified Body*, *Stomach Sculpture*. Brian Massumi argues the body becomes Stelarc's "medium." His "obsolete body" concept provides counterpoint to Cybroc: Stelarc augments the human body because he considers it inadequate; Cybroc augments a vegetable -- satirizing the logic of enhancement.

**Heather Dewey-Hagborg** -- *Stranger Visions* (2012--2013). DNA from discarded items → 3D-printed portraits. Ars Electronica Honorary Mention (2015).

**George Gessert** -- breeding hybrid plants as art since late 1970s. *Green Light: Toward an Art of Evolution* (MIT Press, Leonardo Book Series, 2010). The most relevant plant-focused bio-artist precedent for Cybroc.

### Theoretical Frameworks

**Kac, E. (ed.) *Signs of Life: Bio Art and Beyond*. MIT Press, 2007 (Leonardo Book Series).**
First anthology focused exclusively on bio-art. 30+ contributors including Stafford, Thacker, Nelkin. Features Gessert's "Why I Breed Plants," Catts and Zurr's "Semi-Living Art."

**Mitchell, R. *Bioart and the Vitality of Media*. University of Washington Press, 2010.**
First comprehensive theoretical account. Distinguishes "vitalist bioart" (using living materials) vs "prophylactic bioart" (using traditional media to represent biotechnology). Reads bio-art through Kant's disgust, Deleuze's affect, Simondon's individuation.

**Hauser, J. "Bio Art -- Taxonomy of an Etymological Monster." *Ars Electronica 2005 Catalog*. Also: "Biotechnology as Mediality." *Performance Research* 11(3), 2006.**
Theory of "biomediality" -- biological phenomena as media. His concept of "microperformativity" provides vocabulary for the broccoli's mechanically induced performance.

**Da Costa, B. and Philip, K. (eds.) *Tactical Biopolitics*. MIT Press, 2008 (Leonardo Book Series).**
Bio-art within Foucauldian biopolitics. Cybroc as "tactical intervention" revealing biopolitical logic of longevity optimization.

**Yetisen, A.K., Davis, J., et al. "Bioart." *Trends in Biotechnology* 33(12): 724--734, 2015.**
Most comprehensive peer-reviewed survey. Co-authored by Joe Davis himself.

**Vaage, N.S. "What Ethics for Bioart?" *NanoEthics* 10(1): 87--104, 2016.**
How bio-artists navigate ethics committees. Cybroc introduces novel ethical dimension: working with organisms already "condemned" to consumption.

**Aloi, G. (ed.) *Why Look at Plants?* Brill, 2019. Also: *Estado Vegetal* (U Minnesota Press, 2023).**
Maps "botanical emergence" in contemporary art. Connects plant-based performance to Marder's plant philosophy.

## 2. ARTWORKS FOREGROUNDING DECAY AND BIOLOGICAL TIME

**Dieter Roth (1930--1998)** -- primary historical precedent for decay-as-medium. Chocolate, cheese, sausage, yoghurt as materials welcoming transformation. "Extremely interested in the structure of decay -- its form, play of colours, the variations of putrefaction and mould."

**Sam Taylor-Johnson, *Still Life* (2001)** -- 3:44 time-lapse of fruit decomposition referencing Dutch vanitas. Plastic Bic pen remains unchanged -- environmental commentary.

**Jana Sterbak, *Vanitas: Flesh Dress for an Albino Anorectic* (1987)** -- 50 pounds raw steak sewn into dress that desiccates. Connects mortality, consumption, fashion, the anorexic's "misguided attempts to use mind against body."

**Anya Gallaccio** -- *preserve 'beauty'* (1991--2003): red gerberas behind glass panels; glass ostensibly protecting flowers actually accelerated decomposition. *Stroke* (2004): 90 pounds chocolate coating building.

**Adrian Villar Rojas, *The Theater of Disappearance* (2017)** -- MOCA Geffen. Objects "keep on changing over time through growth and decomposition." Exhibition duration as temporal frame.

**Damien Hirst, *The Physical Impossibility of Death in the Mind of Someone Living* (1991)** -- Tiger shark in formaldehyde. Original shark began rotting despite preservation; replaced 2006. Ironically enacts impossibility named in title.

### Scholarly Analyses

**Schinzel, H. "Should We Reproduce the Beauty of Decay?" *Tate Papers* 8, 2007.** Conservation paradox of works designed to decay.

**Goeppinger, C. "Replication and Decay in Damien Hirst's Natural History." *Tate Papers* 8, 2007.** Preservation and decay coexist.

**Rivenc, R. and Roth, K. (eds.) *Living Matter*. Getty Conservation Institute, 2022.** First major publication on conserving bio-art. 24 papers. Museums sometimes euthanize organisms "against the artist's will."

**Vanitas tradition:** 400-year genealogy. Bergstrom (1956), Segal (1988), Petry *Nature Morte* (2013). Cybroc as **"techno-vanitas"** -- memento mori for the optimization age.

## 3. ARTIFICIAL LIFE ART

**Christa Sommerer & Laurent Mignonneau** -- *Interactive Plant Growing* (1992): real plants → virtual plants. *A-Volve* (1994): real-time evolving virtual creatures. Golden Nica Prix Ars Electronica (1994). *The Artwork as a Living System* (Leonardo/MIT Press, 2022). Their virtual plants grow indefinitely; Cybroc's broccoli decays irreversibly.

**Karl Sims** -- "Evolving Virtual Creatures" (SIGGRAPH 1994). *Galapagos* (1997, NTT Tokyo). Creatures exist in perpetuity; biological death absent from their evolutionary dynamics.

**Theo Jansen** -- Strandbeesten ("beach animals") since 1990. Wind-powered kinetic PVC sculptures. Aspires to make them survive independently -- paralleling longevity aspirations; Cybroc's are doomed.

### Key References

**Whitelaw, M. *Metacreation: Art and Artificial Life*. MIT Press, 2004.** First critical account. Four techniques: Breeders, Cybernatures, Hardware, Abstract Machines.

**Penny, S. "Twenty Years of Artificial Life Art." *Digital Creativity* 21(3), 2010.** "Aesthetics of behavior" framework.

**Wu, Z.-W., Qu, H., and Zhang, K. "A Survey of Recent Practice of Artificial Life in Visual Art." *Artificial Life* 30(1): 106--135, 2024 (MIT Press).** Most recent comprehensive survey.

**Moreno, A. "Artificial Life and Philosophy." *Leonardo* 35(4): 401--405, 2002.** Published in target journal.

**Bureaud, A., Malina, R., and Whiteley, L. (eds.) *Meta-Life*. Leonardo/MIT Press e-book.** 45+ Leonardo articles on ALife art.

**Langton, C. (ed.) *Artificial Life: An Overview*. MIT Press, 1995.** "Life as it could be" vs "life as we know it."

### THE DECAY TABOO IN ALIFE

**Greenfield, R. and Cao, S. "The Edge of Life-as-We-Know-It." *Technoetic Arts* 19(1--2), 2021.**
**SINGLE MOST DIRECTLY RELEVANT ARTICLE.** ALife has privileged emergence while **largely avoiding decay and death.** Identifies a **"decay taboo."** Reframes decay as constructive transformation. Five new tendencies: deep time simulation, slime intelligence, molecular agents, techno resurrection, ecohybridized computation.

**Todd, P.M. "Artificial Death." *ECAL-93 Proceedings*, 1993.** Rare early exception. The tension has been identified but largely ignored for 30+ years.

**Dorin, A. "Artificial Life, Death and Epidemics." *EvoWorkshops 2005*, LNCS 3449: 448--457.** Death in evolutionary computational art via virtual diseases.

**Hayles, N.K. "Narratives of Artificial Life." *FutureNatural*, Routledge, 1996, pp. 146--164.** Critiques ALife's privileging of form over matter. Langton's claim that "aliveness will be found to be a property of logical form" enacts Platonic erasure of the body. **Cybroc reasserts the material body against ALife's dematerializing tendencies.**

## 4. JAPANESE AESTHETICS -- KYOTO CONTEXT

**Wabi-sabi**: beauty of things imperfect, impermanent, incomplete. "The antithesis of our classical Western notion of beauty as something perfect, enduring, and monumental" (Koren).

**Mono no aware**: pathos of things, sensitivity to transience (cherry blossoms). Theorized by Motoori Norinaga (18th c.).

**Mujō**: Buddhist impermanence. Dōgen's *uji* (being-time): full realization of impermanence = Buddha-nature.

### Key References

**Koren, L. *Wabi-Sabi*. Imperfect Publishing, 1994/2008.** Foundational English-language intro.

**Juniper, A. *Wabi Sabi*. Tuttle, 2003.** Accessible bridge for Western readers.

**Parkes, G. "Japanese Aesthetics." *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*, 2023.** Authoritative philosophical reference.

**Saito, Y. *Everyday Aesthetics*. OUP, 2008. Also: "Moral Dimension of Japanese Aesthetics." *JAAC* 65(1), 2007.** Japanese aesthetic tradition as "morally based, promoting respect, care, and consideration for others, both humans and nonhumans."

**Wilkinson, D.J.C. "Wabi-sabi -- a Virtue of Imperfection." *J. Medical Ethics*, 2022. DOI: 10.1136/medethics-2021-108074.** Most directly relevant bridge between wabi-sabi and Western health/mortality debates. Published by Oxford ethicist.

**Hull, M.S. "Mujo." *Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy*, 1998.** Japanese engagement with impermanence "without appeal to a metaphysics of timeless or eternal being."

**Kenkō. *Essays in Idleness*. c. 1330. Trans. Keene, Columbia UP, 1967.** "Branches about to blossom or gardens strewn with faded flowers are worthier of our admiration."

**LITERATURE GAP:** No existing scholarship directly connects Japanese aesthetic philosophy to contemporary bio-art practice or to Western longevity/transhumanism debates. Wilkinson comes closest but doesn't address art.

## 5. COMPARABLE ARTWORKS

### Most Directly Comparable

1. **Sareen & Maes, *Elowan* (2018, MIT Media Lab):** Plant-robot hybrid. Electrodes detect bioelectrical signals → wheeled robot toward light. CHI 2019 Extended Abstracts. *Elowan respects plant agency; Cybroc imposes human protocols.*

2. **Goldberg et al., *Telegarden* (1995--2004):** Web users remotely planted/watered real garden. *The Robot in the Garden* (MIT Press, 2000). *Telegarden was care-oriented; Cybroc imposes optimization.*

3. **Catts & Zurr, *Victimless Leather* (2004):** Semi-living jacket that died at MoMA. *Both works: death of biological material integral to meaning.*

4. **Sommerer & Mignonneau, *Interactive Plant Growing* (1992):** *Digital immortality vs biological mortality.*

5. **Sun Yuan & Peng Yu, *Can't Help Myself* (2016, Guggenheim):** Robotic arm sweeping blood-like fluid, eventually slowing/failing. Viral on TikTok. *Both: mechanical action cannot prevent entropic decline.*

6. **Theo Jansen, Strandbeesten (1990--):** *Aspire to live forever (like transhumanism); Cybroc's broccoli cannot.*

### Novelty Statement
**No existing artwork combines all of Cybroc's elements:** bio-art decay + longevity culture critique + ALife conference context + Japanese impermanence aesthetics + kinetic performance + satirical framing. This intersection is genuinely novel.
